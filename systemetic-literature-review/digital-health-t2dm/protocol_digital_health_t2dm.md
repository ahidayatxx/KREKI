## SECTION 2: BACKGROUND AND RATIONALE

### 2.1 Background Context

#### 2.1.1 Global and National Diabetes Burden

Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) represents one of the most significant public health challenges globally, with the International Diabetes Federation estimating 537 million adults living with diabetes worldwide in 2021, projected to reach 783 million by 2045. Indonesia faces a particularly critical situation as the country with the 5th highest number of people with diabetes globally (approximately 19.5 million adults), with prevalence continuing to rise due to urbanization, dietary changes, and sedentary lifestyles.

**Key Challenges in Indonesian Diabetes Care:**
- Limited access to healthcare services, particularly in rural and remote areas
- Healthcare workforce shortages, especially endocrinologists and diabetes educators
- High out-of-pocket healthcare costs affecting treatment adherence
- Geographic barriers across the archipelago's 17,000+ islands
- Low diabetes awareness and health literacy
- Suboptimal glycemic control among diagnosed patients
- Increasing healthcare system burden

#### 2.1.2 Digital Health as a Solution

Digital health interventions—including mobile health (mHealth) applications, telemedicine platforms, remote monitoring systems, and web-based disease management programs—have emerged as promising tools to overcome traditional healthcare barriers. These technologies offer potential advantages including:

- **Accessibility:** Reaching patients in remote and underserved areas
- **Scalability:** Delivering care to large populations cost-effectively
- **Continuity:** Enabling ongoing patient-provider communication
- **Self-management support:** Empowering patients with tools and information
- **Data integration:** Facilitating real-time monitoring and clinical decision support

Indonesia has demonstrated strong commitment to digital health transformation through the National Digital Health Strategy and increasing smartphone penetration (77% in 2023), creating a conducive environment for digital diabetes interventions.

#### 2.1.3 Current Evidence Landscape

**Global Evidence:**
Systematic reviews of digital health interventions for diabetes management in general populations have shown:
- Moderate improvements in glycemic control (HbA1c reductions of 0.3-0.5%)
- Enhanced medication adherence and self-management behaviors
- Variable effectiveness depending on intervention design and implementation
- Need for context-specific evaluation due to healthcare system differences

**Indonesian Context Gap:**
Despite growing implementation of digital health interventions in Indonesia, several knowledge gaps exist:
- **Limited synthesis:** No comprehensive systematic review specifically examining effectiveness in Indonesian populations and healthcare contexts
- **Heterogeneous evidence:** Existing studies vary widely in intervention types, outcomes, and quality
- **Implementation uncertainty:** Unclear which intervention characteristics are most effective in Indonesian settings
- **Generalizability concerns:** Uncertainty about applicability of international evidence to Indonesian healthcare system

#### 2.1.4 Theoretical Framework

This review is grounded in several complementary theoretical frameworks:

**Implementation Science Framework:**
- RE-AIM (Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance) framework for evaluating intervention success across multiple dimensions
- Consideration of contextual factors affecting implementation in Indonesian healthcare settings

**Chronic Care Model:**
- Digital health as enabler of productive patient-provider interactions
- Support for self-management and clinical decision-making
- Integration with healthcare delivery system organization

**Technology Acceptance Model:**
- User acceptance and engagement as critical determinants of effectiveness
- Cultural and contextual factors influencing technology adoption

#### 2.1.5 Policy and Practice Implications

**National Strategic Alignment:**
This review aligns with Indonesia's national health priorities:
- National Digital Health Strategy (2021-2024)
- Non-Communicable Disease (NCD) prevention and control programs
- Universal Health Coverage (JKN) expansion
- Telemedicine regulations and guidelines

**Stakeholder Relevance:**
Findings will inform multiple stakeholder groups:
- **Ministry of Health:** Evidence for policy development and resource allocation
- **Healthcare providers:** Guidance on intervention selection and implementation
- **Digital health developers:** User needs and effectiveness benchmarks
- **Patients:** Information on available and effective self-management tools
- **Researchers:** Identification of evidence gaps and research priorities

### 2.2 Rationale for Systematic Review

- Aggregate effectiveness evidence across multiple studies
- Assess consistency of findings across different intervention types
- Evaluate quality of existing evidence
- Identify optimal intervention characteristics
- Determine implementation feasibility and scalability

**Methodological Necessity:**
A systematic review is the most appropriate methodology because:
- ✅ Multiple independent studies are available for synthesis
- ✅ Studies have comparable populations (Indonesian adults with T2DM)
- ✅ Common outcome measures exist (HbA1c, clinical parameters)
- ✅ Both statistical (meta-analysis) and narrative synthesis are feasible
- ✅ Question requires comprehensive evidence aggregation, not exploration

**Timing Justification:**
This review is timely because:
- Rapid growth in digital health implementation post-COVID-19 pandemic
- Sufficient primary studies now available for meaningful synthesis
- Policy decisions require evidence-based guidance
- Uncertainty about optimal intervention approaches for Indonesian context

## SECTION 3: OBJECTIVES AND RESEARCH QUESTIONS

### 3.1 Primary Research Question

**Structured Research Question (PICO Framework):**

**"What is the effectiveness of digital health interventions compared to standard care in improving glycemic control among Indonesian adults with Type 2 diabetes mellitus?"**

#### PICO Component Specification:

**P (Population):**
- Indonesian adults (≥18 years of age)
- Diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes mellitus
- Receiving care in Indonesian healthcare settings (hospitals, primary care clinics, community health centers)
- Any diabetes duration
- Both urban and rural populations
- Regardless of baseline glycemic control status

**I (Intervention):**
Digital health interventions for diabetes management, including but not limited to:
- **Mobile health (mHealth) applications** for diabetes self-management
- **Telemedicine platforms** enabling remote consultations with healthcare providers
- **Web-based diabetes management systems** with educational and monitoring components
- **Remote glucose monitoring devices and systems** with data transmission capabilities
- **Digital medication management** and reminder systems
- **Integrated digital health platforms** combining multiple intervention components
- **SMS-based** diabetes education and support programs

*Minimum Intervention Criteria:* Interactive digital component specifically designed for diabetes management (passive information websites excluded)

**C (Comparison):**
- Standard diabetes care per Indonesian clinical practice guidelines
- Usual care without digital health intervention
- Control groups receiving face-to-face care only
- Waitlist control groups
- Attention control groups (if receiving comparable non-digital support)

**O (Outcomes):**

**Primary Outcome:**
- Glycemic control measured by **Glycated Hemoglobin (HbA1c)** levels
  - Expressed as percentage (%) or mmol/mol
  - Change from baseline to follow-up
  - Any follow-up timepoint (3, 6, 12 months or longer)
  - Clinical significance threshold: ≥0.5% reduction

**Secondary Outcomes:**
- Clinical parameters (fasting blood glucose, blood pressure, BMI, lipid profile)
- Behavioral outcomes (medication adherence, self-monitoring frequency)
- Patient-centered outcomes (quality of life, patient satisfaction, healthcare utilization)
- Implementation outcomes (user engagement, acceptability, feasibility)

### 3.2 Secondary Research Questions

**Question 2: Behavioral and Self-Management Outcomes**
"What is the effect of digital health interventions on diabetes self-management behaviors and medication adherence among Indonesian adults with Type 2 diabetes?"

*Purpose:* Assess intervention impact on patient behaviors critical to diabetes control
*Analysis Plan:* Narrative synthesis of self-management measures; meta-analysis if standardized adherence measures used

**Question 3: Patient-Centered Outcomes**
"How do digital health interventions affect quality of life, patient satisfaction, and healthcare utilization among Indonesian adults with Type 2 diabetes?"

*Purpose:* Evaluate patient-relevant outcomes beyond clinical parameters
*Analysis Plan:* Narrative synthesis using established quality of life frameworks; descriptive analysis of utilization patterns

**Question 4: Implementation and Feasibility**
"What are the implementation outcomes (acceptability, feasibility, and sustainability) of digital health interventions for Type 2 diabetes management in Indonesian healthcare settings?"

*Purpose:* Inform scalability and real-world implementation strategies
*Analysis Plan:* Thematic synthesis of implementation barriers, facilitators, and contextual factors using RE-AIM framework

**Question 5: Differential Effectiveness**
"How does the effectiveness of digital health interventions vary by intervention type, healthcare setting, and patient characteristics in the Indonesian context?"

*Purpose:* Identify optimal intervention characteristics and target populations
*Analysis Plan:* Planned subgroup analyses (meta-regression if sufficient studies); comparative effectiveness synthesis

### 3.3 Review Objectives Hierarchy

#### PRIMARY OBJECTIVE

**To systematically evaluate and synthesize evidence on the clinical effectiveness of digital health interventions for Type 2 diabetes management in Indonesian healthcare settings, with primary focus on glycemic control outcomes.**

**Success Criteria:**
- Comprehensive identification of all relevant studies through systematic search
- Meta-analysis of HbA1c outcomes (if ≥3 studies with comparable data available)
- Calculation of pooled effect sizes with 95% confidence intervals
- Assessment of statistical heterogeneity using I² statistic
- Evaluation of clinical significance using ≥0.5% HbA1c reduction threshold
- GRADE assessment of overall evidence quality

#### SECONDARY OBJECTIVES

**Objective 1: Comprehensive Effectiveness Assessment**
To evaluate the effectiveness of digital health interventions on secondary clinical outcomes including:
- Fasting blood glucose levels (mg/dL or mmol/L)
- Medication adherence rates (percentage or validated scales)
- Blood pressure control (systolic/diastolic mmHg) for patients with comorbid hypertension
- Body Mass Index (BMI) and weight management (kg)
- Lipid profile improvements (total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides in mg/dL)

**Success Criteria:**
- Systematic extraction of all reported secondary outcomes
- Meta-analysis or narrative synthesis as appropriate for each outcome
- Consistency assessment across studies

**Objective 2: Behavioral and Self-Management Impact**
To assess the impact of digital health interventions on:
- Diabetes self-management behaviors and knowledge scores
- Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG) frequency and accuracy
- Dietary adherence and nutritional management
- Physical activity levels (minutes/week or similar measures)
- Healthcare engagement and appointment adherence rates

**Success Criteria:**
- Thematic synthesis of behavioral outcomes
- Quantitative pooling if standardized measures used
- Framework synthesis using diabetes self-management models

**Objective 3: Patient-Centered Outcomes Evaluation**
To examine the effect on patient-centered outcomes including:
- Health-related quality of life (using validated instruments: SF-36, DQOL, etc.)
- Patient satisfaction with diabetes care and digital interventions
- Healthcare utilization patterns (clinic visits, emergency department use)
- Diabetes-related hospitalizations and costs (if available)

**Success Criteria:**
- Comprehensive extraction of all patient-reported outcomes
- Narrative synthesis using patient-centered frameworks
- Cost-effectiveness analysis if sufficient economic data available

**Objective 4: Implementation Science Assessment**
To analyze implementation outcomes in Indonesian healthcare contexts:
- Intervention acceptability among patients and providers
- Technical feasibility and infrastructure requirements
- User engagement rates and retention patterns
- Barriers and facilitators to successful implementation
- Sustainability and scalability considerations

**Success Criteria:**
- Thematic analysis using RE-AIM or CFIR frameworks
- Identification of context-specific implementation factors
- Development of evidence-based implementation recommendations

**Objective 5: Heterogeneity and Subgroup Analysis**
To explore sources of heterogeneity and conduct subgroup analyses by:

**Intervention Characteristics:**
- Intervention type (mHealth apps vs. telemedicine vs. integrated platforms)
- Intervention intensity (high vs. low frequency of interaction)
- Intervention components (education, monitoring, reminders, feedback)
- Technology platform (mobile app, web-based, SMS, integrated)

**Healthcare Context:**
- Healthcare setting (primary care vs. hospital-based vs. community)
- Geographic location (urban vs. rural implementation)
- Healthcare system level (public vs. private facilities)

**Patient Characteristics:**
- Age groups (18-40, 41-60, >60 years)
- Diabetes duration (<5 years, 5-10 years, >10 years)
- Baseline glycemic control (well-controlled vs. poorly controlled)
- Comorbidity status (diabetes only vs. multiple comorbidities)
- Socioeconomic factors (if reported)

**Study Design:**
- Study quality (low, moderate, high risk of bias)
- Study design (RCTs vs. quasi-experimental vs. observational)
- Follow-up duration (short-term ≤6 months vs. long-term >6 months)

**Success Criteria:**
- Subgroup analyses conducted if ≥10 studies available
- Meta-regression to explore heterogeneity sources
- Narrative description of patterns across study characteristics

#### EXPLORATORY OBJECTIVES

**Objective 6: Quality of Evidence Assessment**
To evaluate the overall quality and certainty of evidence using GRADE methodology and assess:
- Risk of bias across included studies using appropriate tools
- Consistency of findings across studies and settings
- Directness of evidence to Indonesian populations and settings
- Precision of effect estimates (confidence interval width)
- Publication bias assessment using funnel plots and Egger's test

**Objective 7: Implementation Recommendations Development**
To develop evidence-based recommendations for:
- Optimal digital health intervention characteristics for Indonesian context
- Healthcare system integration strategies and infrastructure needs
- Resource allocation priorities and cost considerations
- Training and capacity building requirements
- Monitoring and evaluation frameworks
- Future research priorities and critical knowledge gaps

**Objective 8: Temporal Effectiveness Analysis**
To examine intervention effectiveness over time (if sufficient data permits):
- Short-term outcomes (≤6 months follow-up)
- Medium-term outcomes (6-12 months follow-up)
- Long-term outcomes (>12 months follow-up)
- Sustainability of intervention effects after discontinuation
- Optimal intervention duration for sustained benefits

### 3.4 Outcome Measures Specification

#### PRIMARY OUTCOME MEASURE

**Glycated Hemoglobin (HbA1c):**
- **Measurement Units:** Percentage (%) or mmol/mol with conversion formula documented
- **Assessment Type:** Change from baseline to post-intervention or absolute values at follow-up
- **Time Points:** All reported time points will be extracted (3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 24 months or other)
- **Clinical Significance:** Minimum clinically important difference = 0.5% reduction
- **Data Extraction:** Mean change, standard deviation, 95% CI, or data enabling calculation
- **Priority:** Primary outcome for meta-analysis if sufficient comparable data

#### SECONDARY OUTCOME MEASURES

**A. Clinical Measures:**

1. **Fasting Blood Glucose (FBG):**
   - Units: mg/dL or mmol/L
   - Normal range: 70-100 mg/dL (3.9-5.6 mmol/L)
   - Extraction: Mean, SD, change from baseline

2. **Random/Postprandial Blood Glucose:**
   - Units: mg/dL or mmol/L
   - Extraction: Mean, SD at specified post-meal intervals

3. **Blood Pressure:**
   - Units: mmHg (systolic/diastolic)
   - Target: <140/90 mmHg for diabetes patients
   - Extraction: Separate systolic and diastolic values

4. **Body Mass Index (BMI):**
   - Units: kg/m²
   - Categories: Normal (<25), Overweight (25-30), Obese (>30)
   - Extraction: Mean BMI and/or weight in kg

5. **Lipid Profile:**
   - Total cholesterol (mg/dL or mmol/L)
   - LDL cholesterol (mg/dL or mmol/L)
   - HDL cholesterol (mg/dL or mmol/L)
   - Triglycerides (mg/dL or mmol/L)

**B. Behavioral and Self-Management Measures:**

1. **Medication Adherence:**
   - Percentage adherence or validated scales (MMAS, MARS, etc.)
   - Self-reported or pharmacy refill data
   - Binary outcomes (adherent vs. non-adherent)

2. **Self-Monitoring Blood Glucose (SMBG) Frequency:**
   - Number of tests per day/week
   - Percentage of patients performing regular SMBG

3. **Diabetes Self-Management:**
   - Validated instruments (SDSCA, DSMQ, DSME-Q)
   - Individual domain scores if available

4. **Dietary Adherence:**
   - Adherence to dietary recommendations (percentage or scale)
   - Nutritional intake measures if available

5. **Physical Activity:**
   - Minutes per week
   - WHO-recommended activity levels achievement

**C. Patient-Centered Measures:**

1. **Health-Related Quality of Life:**
   - Generic instruments (SF-36, SF-12, EQ-5D)
   - Diabetes-specific (DQOL, ADDQoL)
   - Domain scores and total scores

2. **Patient Satisfaction:**
   - Treatment satisfaction (DTSQ, PSQ)
   - Technology satisfaction/usability scores

3. **Healthcare Utilization:**
   - Number of clinic visits
   - Emergency department visits
   - Hospitalizations (diabetes-related)
   - Healthcare costs (if available)

**D. Implementation Outcomes:**

1. **User Engagement:**
   - App usage frequency (logins per week/month)
   - Feature utilization rates
   - Retention rates (percentage completing intervention)

2. **Acceptability:**
   - User satisfaction scores
   - Perceived usefulness and ease of use
   - Qualitative feedback themes

3. **Feasibility:**
   - Technical success rates
   - Infrastructure requirements
   - Implementation barriers and facilitators

4. **Cost and Resource Use:**
   - Intervention costs
   - Cost per patient
   - Cost-effectiveness ratios (if available)

### 4.2 Eligibility Criteria

#### 4.2.1 Inclusion Criteria (Detailed Specification)

**POPULATION:**

**Inclusion:**
- ✅ Adults aged ≥18 years
- ✅ Diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes mellitus (using any recognized diagnostic criteria: ADA, WHO, IDF, or clinical diagnosis)
- ✅ Receiving care in Indonesian healthcare settings (hospitals, community health centers/Puskesmas, primary care clinics, private practices)
- ✅ Indonesian nationals or residents
- ✅ Any diabetes duration (newly diagnosed to long-standing)
- ✅ Any baseline glycemic control status (well-controlled to poorly controlled)
- ✅ With or without diabetes complications
- ✅ With or without comorbid conditions
- ✅ Both urban and rural populations

**Exclusion:**
- ❌ Type 1 diabetes mellitus
- ❌ Gestational diabetes mellitus
- ❌ Secondary diabetes (steroid-induced, pancreatic disease-related)
- ❌ Pediatric populations (<18 years)
- ❌ Studies conducted outside Indonesia (unless specific Indonesian subgroup data extractable)
- ❌ Pre-diabetes or impaired glucose tolerance populations only

**INTERVENTION:**

**Inclusion - Digital Health Interventions Defined As:**
- ✅ **Mobile health (mHealth) applications:**
  - Smartphone or tablet applications for diabetes self-management
  - Features may include glucose logging, medication reminders, education, coaching
  - Standalone apps or integrated with devices

- ✅ **Telemedicine platforms:**
  - Remote consultations with healthcare providers via video, phone, or messaging
  - Virtual clinics for diabetes management
  - Store-and-forward telemedicine for glucose data review

- ✅ **Web-based diabetes management systems:**
  - Interactive websites for diabetes education and self-management
  - Web portals for patient-provider communication
  - Online diabetes coaching or counseling programs

- ✅ **Remote glucose monitoring systems:**
  - Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) with data transmission
  - Connected glucometers with automated data upload
  - Remote monitoring with healthcare provider feedback

- ✅ **Digital medication management:**
  - Electronic medication reminder systems
  - Digital pill boxes with adherence tracking
  - SMS-based medication prompts

- ✅ **Integrated digital health platforms:**
  - Combinations of multiple digital components
  - Ecosystem approaches integrating various digital tools

- ✅ **SMS/text messaging interventions:**
  - Educational messages
  - Behavioral nudges and reminders
  - Two-way SMS communication with providers

**Minimum Intervention Criteria:**
- Must have interactive digital component specifically designed for diabetes management
- Must be delivered primarily through digital technology (not face-to-face with digital supplement only)
- Must have diabetes management as primary or significant focus

**Intervention Exclusion:**
- ❌ Passive information websites without interactive components
- ❌ General health apps without diabetes-specific features
- ❌ Technology solely for healthcare provider use (EHR systems) without patient interface
- ❌ Traditional in-person care with minimal digital augmentation
- ❌ Digital interventions for diabetes prevention only (pre-diabetes)
- ❌ Purely educational materials in digital format without interactive elements

**COMPARISON:**

**Inclusion:**
- ✅ Standard diabetes care per Indonesian clinical practice guidelines
- ✅ Usual care without digital health intervention component
- ✅ Face-to-face diabetes education and management only
- ✅ Waitlist control groups (delayed intervention)
- ✅ Attention control (comparable time and attention without active digital intervention)
- ✅ Alternative non-digital intervention (for comparative effectiveness)
- ✅ No intervention/placebo for behavioral components

**Comparison Exclusion:**
- ❌ No comparator group (single-arm studies)
- ❌ Historical controls without concurrent comparison
- ❌ Comparison group also receiving digital health intervention (unless comparative effectiveness study)

**OUTCOMES:**

**Primary Outcome Inclusion:**
- ✅ Studies reporting glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) as primary or secondary outcome
- ✅ Any follow-up duration (minimum 8 weeks to allow HbA1c change)
- ✅ Reported as percentage or mmol/mol
- ✅ Baseline and post-intervention values, or change scores

**Secondary Outcome Inclusion:**
- ✅ Any of the specified secondary outcomes (clinical, behavioral, patient-centered, implementation)
- ✅ Validated measurement instruments preferred but not required
- ✅ Self-reported or objectively measured outcomes

**Outcome Exclusion:**
- ❌ Studies reporting only process measures without patient outcomes
- ❌ Studies with <8 weeks follow-up (insufficient for HbA1c change detection)
- ❌ Studies reporting outcomes incompatible with synthesis (unique measures)

**STUDY DESIGN:**

**Inclusion:**
- ✅ **Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs):**
  - Individual randomization
  - Cluster randomization (e.g., clinic-level)
  - Parallel group, crossover, or factorial designs

- ✅ **Quasi-Experimental Studies:**
  - Non-randomized controlled trials
  - Controlled before-after studies
  - Interrupted time series (if appropriate comparison)
  - Matched cohort studies

- ✅ **Observational Studies with Comparison Group:**
  - Prospective cohort studies with comparator
  - Retrospective cohort with appropriate controls
  - Case-control studies (if outcome appropriate)

**Study Design Exclusion:**
- ❌ Case series without control group
- ❌ Case reports
- ❌ Cross-sectional studies without baseline measurements
- ❌ Purely descriptive studies
- ❌ Qualitative studies without quantitative outcomes (may be included for implementation objective)
- ❌ Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, narrative reviews (will screen references)
- ❌ Conference abstracts without full-text availability
- ❌ Protocols, study design papers without results

**OTHER ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA:**

**Publication Type:**
- ✅ Peer-reviewed journal articles
- ✅ Grey literature (government reports, dissertations, institutional reports) if meeting methodological standards
- ✅ Conference proceedings if sufficient methodological detail
- ❌ Conference abstracts only (without available full data)
- ❌ Letters, editorials, commentaries

**Language:**
- ✅ English language publications
- ✅ Bahasa Indonesia language publications
- ⚠️ Other languages with available translation resources (case-by-case)

**Publication Date:**
- ✅ January 2015 to search date (October 2025)
- **Rationale:** Digital health technologies for diabetes evolved significantly post-2015; captures modern smartphone-based interventions

**Publication Status:**
- ✅ Published articles
- ✅ In-press articles with DOI
- ✅ Unpublished dissertations/theses from Indonesian universities
- ✅ Government/institutional reports
- ❌ Ongoing studies without results
- ❌ Protocols without outcome data

**Geographic Setting:**
- ✅ Studies conducted entirely in Indonesia
- ✅ Multi-country studies with Indonesian subgroup data extractable
- ❌ Studies conducted outside Indonesia without Indonesian population

**Sample Size:**
- ✅ No minimum sample size requirement for inclusion
- ⚠️ Sample size will be considered in quality assessment and sensitivity analyses

#### 4.2.2 Exclusion Criteria Summary

**Clear Exclusions:**
1. **Population:** Type 1 DM, gestational DM, pediatric populations, non-Indonesian settings
2. **Intervention:** Passive websites, non-diabetes-specific apps, provider-only technologies
3. **Comparison:** Single-arm studies, both groups receiving digital intervention
4. **Outcomes:** <8 weeks follow-up, no HbA1c or relevant outcomes reported
5. **Study Design:** Case reports, case series, cross-sectional, purely qualitative, reviews
6. **Other:** Non-English/non-Indonesian without translation, pre-2015 publications

**Handling of Borderline Cases:**
- Two reviewers will independently assess
- Third reviewer consultation for unresolved disagreements
- Documentation of rationale for borderline inclusion/exclusion decisions
- Conservative approach: "When in doubt, include for full-text review"

**Duplicate Publication Handling:**
- Multiple publications from same study: Most complete data source will be primary; supplementary data extracted from secondary publications
- Companion papers: Linked and cited together; data not double-counted
- Updated analyses: Most recent publication with longest follow-up used

### 4.4 Study Selection Process

#### 4.4.1 Selection Workflow Overview

**Phase 1: Pre-Screening (Deduplication)**
- Import all search results into reference management software
- Automated deduplication using Endnote/Zotero/Mendeley
- Manual verification of duplicate removal
- Record number of duplicates removed

**Phase 2: Title and Abstract Screening**
- Two independent reviewers screen all unique records
- Liberal inclusion approach ("when in doubt, include for full-text review")
- Standardized screening form with eligibility checklist
- Conflict resolution through discussion; third reviewer if needed
- Inter-rater reliability assessment (Cohen's Kappa)

**Phase 3: Full-Text Review**
- Retrieve full-text articles for all potentially eligible studies
- Two independent reviewers assess full eligibility
- Document specific reasons for exclusion
- Resolve discrepancies through discussion; third reviewer consultation
- Inter-rater agreement calculation

**Phase 4: Final Inclusion**
- Create final list of included studies
- Document complete selection process in PRISMA flow diagram
- Maintain detailed record of excluded studies with reasons

#### 4.4.2 Detailed Phase Specifications

**PHASE 1: PRE-SCREENING AND DEDUPLICATION**

**Reference Management:**
- **Software:** Mendeley (or Zotero/EndNote) for citation management
- **Screening Software:** Covidence or DistillerSR for systematic screening
- **Backup:** Excel/Google Sheets for manual tracking if needed

**Deduplication Process:**
- **Automated Deduplication:** 
  - Import all database results into reference manager
  - Run automated duplicate detection based on DOI, title, author, year
  - Flag potential duplicates for manual review
  
- **Manual Verification:**
  - Review flagged duplicates to confirm true duplicates vs. similar studies
  - Check for different publication types of same study (conference + journal article)
  - Retain most complete version; note companion publications
  
- **Documentation:**
  - Record initial total citations retrieved
  - Record number of duplicates removed
  - Note any errors or unusual patterns

**PHASE 2: TITLE AND ABSTRACT SCREENING**

**Screening Team:**
- **Primary Reviewers:** Two independent reviewers (minimum)
- **Reviewer Training:** 
  - Pilot screening of 50 records together
  - Discussion of inclusion criteria application
  - Resolution of discrepancies in pilot
  - Independent screening begins after ≥80% agreement in pilot

**Screening Form Elements:**
```

   ☐ Yes  ☐ No  ☐ Unclear

   ☐ Yes  ☐ No  ☐ Unclear

```

**Screening Approach:**
- **Liberal Inclusion Threshold:** Include if ANY uncertainty exists
- **Exclusion Only When:** Clearly does not meet ≥1 major criterion
- **"Unclear" Handling:** Automatically proceed to full-text review

**Conflict Resolution:**
- **Discrepancy Identification:** Compare independent decisions
- **Discussion:** Two reviewers discuss discrepant records
- **Third Reviewer:** If consensus not reached after discussion
- **Documentation:** Record nature of disagreements and resolution

**Inter-Rater Reliability:**
- **Measure:** Cohen's Kappa statistic calculated for independent screening
- **Target:** Kappa ≥0.60 (substantial agreement)
- **Action if Low:** Additional training and re-screening if Kappa <0.60
- **Reporting:** Kappa value and confidence interval reported in results

#### 4.4.3 Handling Special Cases

**Multi-Country Studies:**
- ✅ Include if Indonesian-specific data can be extracted separately
- ✅ Contact authors for subgroup data if not reported
- ❌ Exclude if Indonesian data cannot be isolated and represent <20% of sample

**Multiple Publications from Same Study:**
- Identify all companion publications (trial registration, protocol, primary results, secondary analyses, long-term follow-up)
- Use primary publication as main data source
- Extract supplementary data from companion papers
- Link all related publications in reference management
- Count as single study in analysis to avoid double-counting participants

**Conference Abstracts vs. Full Publications:**
- Conference abstract alone: ❌ Generally exclude unless exceptional circumstances
- Conference abstract + author-provided data: ✅ Consider on case-by-case basis
- Conference abstract with subsequent full publication: ✅ Include full publication

**Ongoing or Unpublished Studies:**
- Trial registration only without results: ❌ Exclude but note for publication bias assessment
- Author-provided unpublished data: ✅ Include if methodology verifiable
- Dissertation/thesis: ✅ Include if meeting methodological standards

**Language Issues:**
- English or Indonesian: ✅ Include
- Other languages with available translation: ⚠️ Case-by-case; budget permitting
- Non-translatable: ❌ Exclude but note in limitations

**Borderline Intervention Classification:**
- Predominantly digital with minor face-to-face: ✅ Include
- Predominantly face-to-face with digital supplement: ❌ Exclude
- Equal digital and face-to-face components: ⚠️ Discuss with team; likely include with subgroup analysis

### 4.6 Quality Assessment and Risk of Bias

#### 4.6.1 Quality Assessment Tool Selection

**For Randomized Controlled Trials:**

**Tool: Revised Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool (RoB 2)** ✅

**Domains Assessed:**
1. **Bias arising from the randomization process:**
   - Random sequence generation adequacy
   - Allocation concealment
   - Baseline differences between groups

2. **Bias due to deviations from intended interventions:**
   - Blinding of participants and personnel
   - Deviations from intended intervention
   - Appropriate analysis to estimate effect of assignment

3. **Bias due to missing outcome data:**
   - Completeness of outcome data
   - Missing data handling appropriateness
   - Potential for bias from missing data

4. **Bias in measurement of the outcome:**
   - Outcome assessor blinding
   - Measurement method appropriateness
   - Consistency across groups

5. **Bias in selection of reported results:**
   - Pre-specified analysis plan
   - Multiple outcome measurements or analyses
   - Selective reporting of results

**Judgment Categories:**
- ✅ **Low risk of bias**
- ⚠️ **Some concerns**
- ❌ **High risk of bias**

**Overall Risk of Bias:**
- Low: Low risk in all domains
- Some concerns: Some concerns in ≥1 domain
- High: High risk in ≥1 domain OR some concerns in multiple domains

**For Non-Randomized Studies (Quasi-Experimental, Cohort):**

**Tool: ROBINS-I (Risk Of Bias In Non-randomized Studies - of Interventions)** ✅

**Domains Assessed:**
1. **Bias due to confounding:**
   - Baseline confounding control
   - Time-varying confounding

2. **Bias in selection of participants:**
   - Selection into the study
   - Selection of intervention/comparison

3. **Bias in classification of interventions:**
   - Intervention status classification
   - Differential misclassification

4. **Bias due to deviations from intended interventions:**
   - Implementation of intended intervention
   - Deviations and co-interventions

5. **Bias due to missing data:**
   - Missing data completeness
   - Missing data handling

6. **Bias in measurement of outcomes:**
   - Outcome measurement methods
   - Blinding of assessors

7. **Bias in selection of reported results:**
   - Selective outcome reporting
   - Multiple analyses

**Judgment Categories:**
- ✅ **Low risk**
- ⚠️ **Moderate risk**
- ❌❌ **Serious risk**
- ❌❌❌ **Critical risk**
- ❓ **No information**

**For Qualitative Studies (Implementation Outcomes):**

**Tool: CASP Qualitative Checklist (if qualitative data included)** ⚠️

**Sections Assessed:**
1. Validity of research aims
2. Appropriateness of qualitative methodology
3. Research design appropriateness
4. Recruitment strategy
5. Data collection appropriateness
6. Researcher-participant relationship consideration
7. Ethical issues consideration
8. Rigor of data analysis
9. Clear statement of findings

**Homogeneity Assessment Criteria:**

**Clinical Homogeneity:**
- ✅ Similar populations (Indonesian adults with T2DM)
- ✅ Comparable interventions (digital health for diabetes management)
- ✅ Similar comparisons (standard care or usual care)
- ✅ Same outcome definition (HbA1c in % or mmol/mol)

- Similar outcome measurement timing

**Statistical Homogeneity:**
- I² <75% (substantial heterogeneity threshold)
- P-value for Chi² test >0.10 preferable
- Consideration of prediction intervals

#### 4.7.2 Quantitative Synthesis (Meta-Analysis)

**CONDITIONS FOR META-ANALYSIS:**
- Minimum 3 studies with comparable outcome data
- Clinical and methodological homogeneity acceptable
- Sufficient data quality for pooling
- Appropriate statistical models available

**PRIMARY OUTCOME META-ANALYSIS: HbA1c**

**Effect Measure:**
- **Mean Difference (MD)** for HbA1c change from baseline
- Units: Percentage points (%) or mmol/mol
- 95% Confidence Intervals for all estimates
- Direction: Negative MD favors intervention (HbA1c reduction)

**Statistical Model Selection:**

**Random-Effects Model (Primary Analysis):** ✅
- **Method:** DerSimonian and Laird inverse-variance or Restricted Maximum Likelihood (REML)
- **Rationale:** 
  - Assumes true effect varies across studies
  - More conservative than fixed-effect
  - Appropriate given expected heterogeneity in interventions and settings
  - Provides more generalizable estimates

**Fixed-Effect Model (Sensitivity Analysis):** ⚠️
- **Method:** Inverse variance weighting
- **Use:** Sensitivity analysis to assess impact of model choice
- **Comparison:** Compare random-effects vs. fixed-effect results

**Meta-Analysis Procedure:**

**Step 1: Data Preparation**
- Extract or calculate mean difference and SE/SD for each study
- Convert all HbA1c values to consistent units
- Standardize time points (prefer longest follow-up if multiple)
- Check for data entry errors and outliers

**Step 2: Effect Size Calculation**
- Calculate mean difference (MD) or standardized mean difference (SMD) if needed
- Compute 95% confidence intervals
- Calculate variance and standard error

**Step 3: Pooled Estimate**
- Conduct random-effects meta-analysis
- Generate forest plot with individual study estimates
- Calculate pooled mean difference and 95% CI
- Compute prediction interval (95% PI) for expected effect in new setting

**Step 4: Heterogeneity Assessment**

**Statistical Measures:**
- **I² statistic:** Percentage of variation due to heterogeneity
  - 0-40%: Might not be important
  - 30-60%: May represent moderate heterogeneity
  - 50-90%: May represent substantial heterogeneity
  - 75-100%: Considerable heterogeneity
  
- **Tau² (τ²):** Between-study variance
  - Absolute measure of heterogeneity
  
- **Chi² test (Q statistic):** 
  - P <0.10 suggests significant heterogeneity
  
- **Prediction Interval:**
  - Range of true effects in 95% of settings
  - Wider than confidence interval when heterogeneity present

**Heterogeneity Investigation:**
If substantial heterogeneity (I² >50%):
1. Visual inspection of forest plot for outliers
2. Subgroup analyses (see below)
3. Meta-regression (if ≥10 studies)
4. Sensitivity analyses removing studies
5. Consider narrative synthesis if unexplained I² >75%

**SECONDARY OUTCOME META-ANALYSES:**

**Similar Process for:**
- Fasting blood glucose (MD in mg/dL or mmol/L)
- Blood pressure (MD in mmHg, systolic and diastolic separate)
- BMI (MD in kg/m²)
- Medication adherence (if standardized measure)

**Considerations:**
- Fewer studies may be available for secondary outcomes
- Greater heterogeneity in measurement approaches expected
- May require standardized mean difference (SMD) if different scales used

#### 4.7.3 Subgroup and Sensitivity Analyses

**PLANNED SUBGROUP ANALYSES** (if ≥10 studies available):

**1. Intervention Type:**
- Mobile health (mHealth) applications
- Telemedicine/teleconsultation platforms
- SMS-based interventions
- Web-based programs
- Remote monitoring systems
- Integrated/multicomponent platforms

**2. Intervention Intensity:**
- High intensity (daily or multiple times/week interaction)
- Medium intensity (weekly interaction)
- Low intensity (less than weekly or on-demand)

**3. Healthcare Setting:**
- Hospital-based care
- Primary care/community health centers
- Community-based programs
- Home-based with remote support

**4. Geographic Context:**
- Urban settings
- Rural/remote settings
- Specific regions if data permits

**5. Study Quality:**
- Low risk of bias
- Some concerns
- High risk of bias

**6. Follow-Up Duration:**
- Short-term (≤6 months)
- Medium-term (>6 to 12 months)
- Long-term (>12 months)

**7. Patient Characteristics (if data permits):**
- Age groups (18-40, 41-60, >60 years)
- Baseline glycemic control (well-controlled HbA1c <7% vs. poorly controlled ≥7%)
- Diabetes duration (<5 years vs. ≥5 years)

**Subgroup Analysis Methods:**
- Stratified meta-analysis by subgroup
- Test for subgroup differences using Chi² test
- Meta-regression if ≥10 studies (continuous moderators)
- Report interaction p-values
- Interpret with caution; clearly label as exploratory

**SENSITIVITY ANALYSES:**

**1. Study Design:**
- RCTs only (exclude quasi-experimental/observational)
- Individual RCTs only (exclude cluster RCTs)

**2. Risk of Bias:**
- Low risk of bias studies only
- Exclude high risk of bias studies

**3. Statistical Model:**
- Fixed-effect model vs. random-effects model comparison

**4. Outlier Impact:**
- Remove studies with extreme effect sizes
- Remove studies with high influence (e.g., very large sample)

**5. Missing Data:**
- Complete case analysis (available data only)
- Best-case scenario (impute favorable outcomes for missing)
- Worst-case scenario (impute unfavorable outcomes for missing)

**6. Publication Type:**
- Published peer-reviewed articles only
- Include grey literature

**7. Definition Variations:**
- Different time point selections (3m vs 6m vs 12m)
- Different control group definitions

**Sensitivity Analysis Reporting:**
- Present results alongside primary analysis
- Assess impact on effect estimate and statistical significance
- Discuss robustness of primary findings
- Note if conclusions would change

#### 4.7.4 Publication Bias Assessment

**METHODS (if ≥10 studies available):**

**1. Funnel Plot:**
- Visual inspection for asymmetry
- Plot effect size vs. standard error (or sample size)
- Asymmetry suggests possible publication bias or other small-study effects

**2. Egger's Regression Test:**
- Statistical test for funnel plot asymmetry
- P <0.10 suggests significant asymmetry
- Linear regression of effect estimate precision

**3. Trim and Fill Method:**
- Imputes missing studies to assess impact
- Provides adjusted effect estimate
- Identifies potential number of missing studies

**4. Contour-Enhanced Funnel Plot:**
- Adds significance contours to funnel plot
- Helps distinguish publication bias from heterogeneity

**Publication Bias Interpretation:**
- Acknowledge potential for bias if asymmetry detected
- Consider alternative explanations (heterogeneity, true effect variation)
- Discuss impact on confidence in effect estimate
- Downgrade GRADE certainty if serious concern

**Trial Registration Review:**
- Compare published studies to registered trials
- Identify completed but unpublished studies
- Contact authors of unpublished registered trials

**NARRATIVE SYNTHESIS FRAMEWORK:**

**Synthesis Framework Selection:**
Based on Popay et al. (2006) Guidance on Narrative Synthesis:

**1. Developing a Theory:**
- Preliminary synthesis of findings
- Framework development (e.g., logic model of how digital health affects diabetes outcomes)

**2. Developing a Preliminary Synthesis:**

**Textual Description:**
- Systematic description of studies (tabulation)

**Tabulation:**
- Evidence tables summarizing:
  - Study characteristics
  - Population details
  - Intervention descriptions
  - Outcome results
  - Quality assessments
- Organization by intervention type, outcome, or other relevant characteristic

**Vote Counting (Effect Direction):**
- Tally of studies showing:
  - Positive effect (favors intervention)
  - Negative effect (favors control)
  - No significant difference
- Note: Interpret cautiously; does not account for study quality or precision

**3. Exploring Relationships:**

**Within-Study Analysis:**
- Dose-response patterns
- Moderators and mediators
- Subgroup findings

**Between-Study Analysis:**
- Factors associated with greater/lesser effectiveness
- Patterns by intervention characteristics
- Contextual factors influencing outcomes

**4. Assessing Robustness:**
- Quality assessment integration
- Consistency of findings across studies
- Plausibility of findings
- Generalizability assessment

**SYNTHESIS OF IMPLEMENTATION OUTCOMES:**

**Thematic Analysis:**
- Identify barriers and facilitators themes
- Acceptability and feasibility patterns
- User engagement factors
- Contextual influences on implementation

**Framework Synthesis Using RE-AIM:**
- **Reach:** Who received the intervention? Representativeness?
- **Effectiveness:** What were the outcomes?
- **Adoption:** What settings/staff adopted the intervention?
- **Implementation:** Was it delivered as intended? Fidelity?
- **Maintenance:** Were effects sustained? Was intervention sustained?

**Logic Model Development:**
- Map intervention components to mechanisms of action
- Link mechanisms to intermediate and final outcomes
- Identify contextual factors moderating pathways

**QUALITATIVE SYNTHESIS (if applicable):**
- Meta-ethnography or thematic synthesis of qualitative findings
- Integration of qualitative and quantitative findings
- Convergent synthesis approach

#### 4.7.7 GRADE Evidence Quality Assessment

**GRADE (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation):**

**Outcomes to be GRADED:**
- Primary outcome (HbA1c)
- Critical secondary outcomes:
  - Fasting blood glucose
  - Medication adherence
  - Quality of life
  - Healthcare utilization

**GRADE Domains:**

**1. Risk of Bias:**
- Start: High (RCTs) or Low (Observational)
- Downgrade if serious or very serious limitations
- Based on quality assessment (RoB 2 or ROBINS-I)

**2. Inconsistency:**
- Assess heterogeneity of results (I² statistic)
- Downgrade if unexplained substantial heterogeneity
- Consider consistency of effect direction

**3. Indirectness:**
- Population, intervention, comparison, outcome directness
- Indonesian population: Direct
- Digital health interventions: Assess specificity
- Downgrade if significant indirectness

**4. Imprecision:**
- Wide confidence intervals suggesting uncertainty
- Optimal information size not met
- Downgrade if CIs include both benefit and harm

**5. Publication Bias:**
- Evidence of bias from funnel plot, Egger's test
- Missing studies from trial registries
- Downgrade if strong suspicion

**Certainty Levels:**
- ⭐⭐⭐⭐ **High:** Very confident true effect close to estimate
- ⭐⭐⭐ **Moderate:** Moderately confident; true effect likely close to estimate
- ⭐⭐ **Low:** Limited confidence; true effect may be substantially different
- ⭐ **Very Low:** Very little confidence; true effect likely substantially different

**GRADE Summary of Findings Table:**
- Create summary table for main outcomes
- Include:
  - Number of participants and studies
  - Certainty of evidence (quality rating)
  - Relative effect (MD, RR, etc.)
  - Anticipated absolute effects (risk difference)
  - Plain language summary

### 6.4 Risk Management

#### 6.4.1 Potential Risks and Mitigation Strategies

**RISK 1: Insufficient Literature Identified**

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM

**Description:** Fewer than anticipated studies meeting inclusion criteria, preventing meaningful synthesis

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Preliminary scoping:** Conduct thorough preliminary search to estimate literature volume before full protocol
- **Flexible inclusion criteria:** Carefully consider whether criteria are overly restrictive; adjust if appropriate without compromising scientific rigor
- **Broader search:** Expand to regional comparators (ASEAN countries with similar contexts) if absolutely necessary
- **Alternative synthesis:** Prepare for narrative synthesis if meta-analysis not feasible
- **Communication:** If literature truly insufficient, consider scoping review or acknowledge in limitations and focus on research gaps

**Contingency Plan:** If <3 studies identified, pivot to scoping review or narrative review after team discussion; if 3-9 studies, proceed with narrative synthesis and limited meta-analysis

---

**RISK 2: High Heterogeneity Preventing Meta-Analysis**

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM-HIGH

**Description:** Substantial clinical or methodological heterogeneity (I² >75%) makes statistical pooling inappropriate

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Expect heterogeneity:** Plan for narrative synthesis from the outset as complementary approach
- **Subgroup analyses:** Pre-plan subgroup analyses to explore heterogeneity sources
- **Meta-regression:** Use if ≥10 studies to identify moderators
- **Narrative integration:** Robust narrative synthesis can provide valuable insights even without meta-analysis
- **Transparent reporting:** Clearly report heterogeneity and reasons for not pooling if applicable

**Contingency Plan:** If meta-analysis inappropriate due to heterogeneity, focus on high-quality narrative synthesis using established frameworks; report heterogeneity as important finding about intervention variability

---

**RISK 3: Team Member Availability Changes**

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM

**Description:** Key team member becomes unavailable due to competing commitments, leave, or other circumstances

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Clear roles and backup:** Define roles clearly; identify backup reviewers for critical tasks
- **Cross-training:** Ensure at least 3 team members trained on screening and extraction procedures
- **Documentation:** Maintain detailed documentation so others can take over if needed
- **Regular communication:** Weekly or biweekly team meetings to monitor progress and availability
- **Flexible timeline:** Build buffer time into schedule for unexpected delays

**Contingency Plan:** If primary reviewer unavailable, third trained reviewer steps in; if PI unavailable temporarily, co-investigator assumes coordination; if long-term absence, consider timeline extension

---

**RISK 4: Database Access Limitations**

**Risk Level:** LOW-MEDIUM

**Description:** Loss of access to key databases or technical difficulties

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Institutional agreements:** Confirm stable institutional subscriptions before starting
- **Alternative databases:** Identify alternative databases for key sources (e.g., PubMed free vs. MEDLINE Ovid)
- **Early searching:** Conduct database searches early in timeline to address access issues
- **Librarian collaboration:** Work closely with library to ensure stable access

**Contingency Plan:** If database access lost, use free alternatives (PubMed, PMC, Google Scholar) and acknowledge limitations; contact authors directly for articles; use interlibrary loan extensively

---

**RISK 5: Timeline Delays**

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM

**Description:** Project takes longer than anticipated due to volume of studies, extraction complexity, or other factors

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Realistic timeline:** Build in buffer time (10-15% extra) for each phase
- **Progress monitoring:** Track progress weekly against milestones
- **Early problem identification:** Flag delays early and adjust resource allocation
- **Prioritization:** Focus on primary outcome (HbA1c) first; secondary outcomes can follow
- **Flexible deadlines:** Set internal deadlines earlier than external commitments

**Contingency Plan:** If delays occur, reassess resource allocation; consider additional team members for labor-intensive phases; adjust conference presentation plans if needed; maintain quality over speed

---

**RISK 6: Missing or Inadequate Data**

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM

**Description:** Included studies do not report sufficient data for extraction or meta-analysis

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Author contact plan:** Systematic author contact with clear data requests
- **Multiple contact attempts:** Email + ResearchGate/LinkedIn contact if needed
- **Data calculation:** Calculate missing statistics from available data where possible (SD from SE/CI, etc.)
- **Sensitivity analyses:** Test impact of missing data assumptions
- **Transparent reporting:** Clearly report all missing data and handling approaches

**Contingency Plan:** If data remain missing despite author contact, proceed with available data; conduct sensitivity analyses with different missing data assumptions; downgrade GRADE certainty for imprecision if appropriate

---

**RISK 7: Quality of Available Evidence Is Low**

**Risk Level:** LOW-MEDIUM

**Description:** Most or all included studies have high risk of bias

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **No quality exclusion:** Do not exclude studies based on quality; include all and assess quality rigorously
- **Transparent quality reporting:** Report quality honestly and comprehensively
- **GRADE downgrading:** Use GRADE to downgrade certainty for limitations
- **Sensitivity analysis:** Analyze high-quality studies separately if any exist
- **Implications discussion:** Discuss implications for practice and need for higher-quality research

**Contingency Plan:** Low-quality evidence is still valuable to report; focus on identifying research gaps and methodological recommendations for future studies; downgrade GRADE certainty appropriately

---

**RISK 8: Publication During Review Process**

**Risk Level:** LOW

**Description:** Protocol or similar systematic review published by another team during our review

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **PROSPERO registration:** Early registration establishes priority and transparency
- **Regular monitoring:** Monitor for new publications in area throughout review process
- **Rapid completion:** Efficient progression through timeline
- **Unique value:** Emphasize unique Indonesian focus and comprehensive approach

**Contingency Plan:** If similar review published, assess overlap; emphasize unique contributions (Indonesian focus, different outcomes, updated timeline); may publish as update or complementary analysis

---

**RISK 9: Stakeholder Engagement Challenges**

**Risk Level:** LOW

**Description:** Difficulty engaging key stakeholders (Ministry of Health, clinicians, patients) for input or dissemination

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **Early engagement:** Identify and contact stakeholders early in process
- **Multiple channels:** Use multiple communication channels (email, phone, in-person meetings)
- **Flexibility:** Offer flexible meeting times and formats (virtual, in-person)
- **Value proposition:** Clearly communicate value and relevance to stakeholders
- **Partnerships:** Leverage existing relationships and professional networks

**Contingency Plan:** If stakeholder engagement limited during review, focus on evidence synthesis; engage intensively during dissemination phase; work through professional societies and existing networks
