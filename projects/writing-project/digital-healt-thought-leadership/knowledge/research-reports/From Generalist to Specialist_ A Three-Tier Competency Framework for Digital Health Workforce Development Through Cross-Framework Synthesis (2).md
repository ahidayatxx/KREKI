# **From Generalist to Specialist: A Three-Tier Competency Framework for Digital Health Workforce Development Through Cross-Framework Synthesis**

### **By Ahmad Hidayat**

## **Abstract**

The rapid digitalization of the healthcare sector has necessitated a workforce that is not only digitally literate but also stratified according to operational and strategic responsibilities. While existing frameworks such as the Australian Digital Health Capability Framework (ADHCF) and the Australian Health Informatics Competency Framework (AHICF) provide robust standards for generalists and specialists respectively, a structural gap remains regarding the transitional competencies required for operational management and applied practice. This paper presents the theoretical foundation and architectural logic for the development of the "3-Tier Digital Health Competency Framework. By employing integrative cross-framework mapping and structural triangulation—methodological approaches validated in recent competency framework research (MacKay et al., 2024; Stoll et al., 2024)—this framework systematically extracts, analyzes, and synthesizes competencies from the ADHCF and AHICF to create a unified taxonomy. The study culminates in the **3-Tiers Digital Health Competency Mapping and Cross-Framework Alignment**, a unified taxonomy that triangulates workforce needs against cognitive complexity—Foundational Digital Health Literacy (Tier 1), Applied Digital Health Practice (Tier 2), and Strategic Health Informatics Leadership (Tier 3). This alignment provides a scalable logic for workforce development, ensuring safe, seamless, and secure health service delivery while delineating clear career pathways from generalist practice to informatics leadership.

**Keywords:** Digital Health, Competency Framework, Workforce Development, Health Informatics, Cross-Framework Synthesis, ADHCF, AHICF.

## **1\. Introduction**

Digital health is a critical component of the modern healthcare industry, essential for optimizing access to services and enabling new models of care. As recognized in Australia’s National Digital Health Strategy, the health workforce must be capable of confidently using digital health technologies to deliver care. However, the workforce is not monolithic; it ranges from frontline clinicians needing basic literacy to health informaticians designing complex data architectures.

Currently, two primary frameworks govern this domain in Australia:

1. **The Australian Digital Health Capability Framework (ADHCF):** A standard designed for "all individuals who deliver or assist in the delivery of health services," focusing on digital literacy and capability across the broad workforce.  
2. **The Australian Health Informatics Competency Framework (AHICF):** A specialized standard explicitly titled "For Health Informaticians," focusing on information science, health sciences, and information technology at a deeper, theoretical level.

Recent scholarship in competency framework development demonstrates the value of synthesizing multiple authoritative sources to address workforce stratification gaps (Barraclough et al., 2024; MacKay et al., 2024). This approach, termed 'framework synthesis,' enables researchers to construct cohesive developmental models from existing standards while preserving the validity of source frameworks (Stoll et al., 2024). The present study applies this methodology to the Australian digital health context, guided by three objectives: (1) to identify the implicit hierarchical progression embedded within the ADHCF and AHICF, (2) to construct a unified competency taxonomy that preserves cognitive complexity distinctions, and (3) to validate the resulting framework through systematic cross-referencing to source documents.

While these frameworks serve their respective audiences effectively, they exist as distinct islands of competency. There is a lack of a clear structural bridge connecting the "digital user" (generalist) to the "digital architect" (specialist). This paper addresses this disconnect by synthesizing these documents into a single, progressive continuum.

## **2\. Methodology**

The development of the 3-Tier Framework utilizes a methodology of integrative cross-framework mapping and structural triangulation, drawing upon established approaches in competency framework development (MacKay et al., 2024; Stoll et al., 2024). This systematic approach addresses recognized gaps in health workforce competency frameworks by synthesizing multiple authoritative sources into a cohesive developmental model (Barraclough et al., 2024).

Framework synthesis, as a methodological approach, enables researchers to construct new conceptual structures by systematically extracting, analyzing, and recombining elements from existing frameworks (Bexten et al., 2025). This process involves three distinct analytical phases:

### **Phase 1: Audience Segmentation and Alignment**

The target audiences defined in the source frameworks were analyzed to establish a three-tiered hierarchy following principles of hierarchical workforce stratification documented in health systems literature. The ADHCF's "all individuals" audience was mapped to Tier 1\. The AHICF's "Health Informaticians" audience was mapped to Tier 3\. A hybrid audience of "supervisory roles and project leads" was identified for Tier 2, addressing what Shickle et al. (2018) describe as the "practitioner workforce" gap in competency frameworks.

### **Phase 2: Domain Synthesis and Extraction**

Competency domains from the ADHCF (Domains A-E) and AHICF (Domains A-F) were deconstructed through systematic content analysis. Content was extracted and re-categorized based on the requisite level of cognitive complexity, utilizing the Revised Bloom's Taxonomy as the analytical framework (Bexten et al., 2025). This taxonomic approach has demonstrated validity across multiple healthcare education contexts (Shickle et al., 2018).

### **Phase 3: Semantic Normalization and Validation**

The distinct vocabularies of "capability" (ADHCF) and "competency" (AHICF) were harmonized through conceptual alignment. The 3-Tier Framework progresses from "Understanding" (Tier 1\) to "Applying" (Tier 2\) to "Appraising/Analysing" (Tier 3), consistent with validated approaches to competency-based medical education (Himes et al., 2025). Each competency statement was traced back to its source framework to ensure validity and prevent arbitrary categorization.

This methodology reflects best practices in competency framework development research, emphasizing governance structures, systematic extraction, and alignment with educational taxonomies (MacKay et al., 2024; Mocke et al., 2024; Stoll et al., 2024).

## **3\. The 3-Tier Digital Health Competency Framework**

The resulting framework is organized into three distinct tiers representing a career and capability progression.

### **Tier 1: Foundational Digital Health Literacy**

* **Focus:** Digital Professionalism, Tools & Systems, Data Management.  
* **Competency Nature:** Operational and user-centric.  
* **Source Derivation:** Predominantly ADHCF. It summarizes the ADHCF’s five domains into simplified categories for broad consumption. For example, the competence "Understands and adheres to relevant legislation... policy, and ethics" is a direct extraction from ADHCF Domain A (Digital Professionalism).

The theoretical foundation for Tier 1 is predicated on the concept of a 'license to operate' within modern health environments—a minimum viable standard aligned with the National Digital Health Strategy's goal of "safe, seamless, and secure" care delivery. This tier addresses the ADHCF's foundational assertion that 'all health workers play a crucial role in the capture of quality data,' recognizing that higher-level informatics work (Tier 3\) is impossible without accurate data entry at point-of-care (Tier 1). The competencies align with the 'Understanding' and 'Formative' levels of established taxonomies, focusing on comprehension and compliance rather than analysis or creation.

### **Tier 2: Applied Digital Health Practice**

* **Focus:** Implementation, Change Management, Workflow Optimization.  
* **Competency Nature:** Tactical and managerial.  
* **Source Derivation:** A hybrid synthesis. It elevates ADHCF concepts to a management level (e.g., moving from "using" data to "validating" data quality) and pulls operational competencies from the AHICF (e.g., project management) that are relevant to non-informatician leaders.

The theoretical foundation for Tier 2 addresses what Shickle et al. (2018) identify as the "practitioner workforce" gap in competency frameworks. This tier recognizes that digital health transformation requires middle-management professionals—clinical leaders, service managers, and project leads—who can operationalize informatics concepts without requiring the deep theoretical training of specialists. The competencies align with the "Applying" and "Intermediate" levels of Bloom's Taxonomy, emphasizing procedural execution and analytical problem-solving rather than architectural design. This tier bridges the conceptual gap between system usage (Tier 1\) and system governance (Tier 3), providing the operational leadership necessary for sustainable digital transformation.

This mapping reveals the framework's primary methodological innovation: the explicit articulation of management-level competencies that neither source framework addresses independently. The progression from "Plans" (E1) to "Analyses" (F1) to "Implements" (G1) to "Leads" (H1) demonstrates the operational leadership trajectory required for digital health transformation. These competencies synthesize ADHCF's "Proficient" level with AHICF's management domains (Leadership & Management, Social & Behavioural Sciences), addressing the "practitioner workforce gap" documented in public health competency research (Shickle et al., 2018).

### **Tier 3: Strategic Health Informatics Leadership**

* **Focus:** Governance, Strategy, Data Science, Innovation.  
* **Competency Nature:** Strategic and theoretical.  
* **Source Derivation:** Predominantly AHICF. This tier retains the high-level cognitive verbs of the AHICF, such as "Appraise," "Differentiate," and "Distinguish," focusing on the theoretical underpinnings of health informatics.

The theoretical foundation for Tier 3 establishes the apex of the competency hierarchy, reserved for professionals who design, govern, and critically evaluate the digital health ecosystem. This tier aligns with the AHICF's emphasis on mastery of "theoretical and conceptual frameworks" and understanding of "the history of health informatics." The competencies correspond to the "Analysing," "Evaluating," and "Creating" levels of Bloom's Taxonomy, requiring professionals to differentiate complex ontologies, appraise interoperability standards, and synthesize evidence to inform strategic decisions. The inclusion of historical and theoretical knowledge distinguishes Tier 3 specialists from Tier 2 practitioners: specialists understand not only how systems operate but why they are structured as they are, enabling evidence-based innovation and governance.

## **4\. The Ultimate Outcome: 3-Tiers Digital Health Competency Mapping and Cross-Framework Alignment**

The culmination of this research is the **3-Tiers Digital Health Competency Mapping and Cross-Framework Alignment**. This mapping validates the framework by explicitly tracing every competency statement back to its roots in either the ADHCF or the AHICF, proving that the 3-Tier model is not a new invention but a logical re-ordering of existing national standards.

The mapping reveals the progression of specific domains across the tiers:

### **4.1 Domain Alignment: Digital Professionalism & Ethics**

* **Tier 1 (Foundational):** The mapping aligns with **ADHCF Domain A (Digital Professionalism)**.  
  * *Competency:* "Understands and adheres to relevant legislation, professional codes of conduct, policy, and ethics."  
  * *Outcome:* This ensures the general workforce operates within legal boundaries.  
* **Tier 3 (Strategic):** The mapping aligns with **AHICF Domain E (Social & Behavioural Sciences)**.  
  * *Competency:* "Interprets the legislative, regulatory, and policy obligations that are relevant in specific digital health contexts."  
  * *Outcome:* The shift from "adhering" (Tier 1\) to "interpreting" (Tier 3\) demonstrates the jump in cognitive complexity required for leadership.

### **4.2 Domain Alignment: Digital Tools & Systems vs. Technology**

* **Tier 1 (Foundational):** Aligns with **ADHCF Domain B (Leadership & Advocacy \- Digital Tools)**.  
  * *Competency:* "Recognises and uses the right technology for the needs of both the patient and the worker."  
* **Tier 3 (Strategic):** Aligns with **AHICF Domain C (Information Technology)**.  
  * *Competency:* "Analyses the technological infrastructure required to support digital health solutions."  
  * *Outcome:* The mapping highlights the transition from *utility* (using the right tool) to *architecture* (designing the infrastructure).

### **4.3 The Cross-Framework Synthesis Table**

The following summary illustrates the high-level alignment established by the mapping:

| 3-Tier Framework Component | Primary Source Alignment | Assessment Level | Cognitive Domain |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Tier 1: Foundational** | **ADHCF** (All Domains) | Formative / Intermediate | Remember / Understand / Apply |
| **Tier 2: Applied** | **ADHCF** (Proficient) \+ **AHICF** (Management) | Proficient / Applied | Apply / Analyze |
| **Tier 3: Strategic** | **AHICF** (All Domains) | Proficient / Expert | Evaluate / Create |

This mapping demonstrates systematic extraction from source frameworks while preserving cognitive complexity distinctions. The progression from 'Understands' (A1, Tier 1\) to 'Interprets' (K2, Tier 3\) within the digital professionalism domain exemplifies the framework's alignment with Bloom's Taxonomy, moving from knowledge comprehension to analytical application.

This cross-framework synthesis demonstrates the progressive cognitive trajectory embedded within the unified framework. The transition from "Remember/Understand/Apply" (Tier 1\) through "Apply/Analyze" (Tier 2\) to "Evaluate/Create" (Tier 3\) reflects established principles of competency-based education that link skill complexity to professional responsibility (Mocke et al., 2024). The hybrid nature of Tier 2—drawing from both ADHCF's proficient level and AHICF's management competencies—exemplifies the framework's methodological contribution: systematic identification and articulation of transitional competencies that enable career progression without requiring immediate specialist training.

## **5\. Conclusion**

### **Theoretical Contributions and Implications**

This framework contributes to competency framework development methodology by demonstrating that synthesis of existing authoritative standards can address workforce stratification gaps without requiring de novo framework creation. The systematic application of Bloom's Taxonomy as an organizing principle for cross-framework mapping extends methodological approaches documented in recent healthcare education literature (Bexten et al., 2025; Mocke et al., 2024).

### **Limitations and Future Research**

This study focuses on Australian frameworks and may require contextual adaptation for other health systems. Future research should empirically validate the framework through Delphi studies with Australian digital health stakeholders and assess its applicability to other national contexts. Additionally, the development of assessment instruments aligned with each tier would enable workforce capability measurement and professional development tracking.

### **Policy Implications**

The framework provides a foundation for national workforce planning, enabling health organizations to conduct competency gap analyses and design targeted professional development pathways. Its alignment with existing national standards facilitates adoption without disrupting current capability frameworks.

This analysis confirms that a seamless developmental pathway for the digital health workforce is achievable without creating new standards from scratch. By synthesizing the ADHCF and AHICF, the **3-Tiers Digital Health Competency Mapping and Cross-Framework Alignment** provides the "missing link" between generalist capability and specialist expertise.

This structure allows healthcare organizations to:

1. **Standardize onboarding** using Tier 1 for all staff.  
2. **Upskill managers** using Tier 2 to handle digital transformation projects.  
3. **Recruit and deploy specialists** using Tier 3 to lead strategy and governance.

The ultimate outcome is a unified ecosystem where every role, from the bedside nurse to the Chief Information Officer, can locate their specific contribution to the digital health strategy within a single, aligned framework.
