# Academic Rationale for the Value-Based Digital Health Innovation Canvas (VDHIC)

**A Theoretical Foundation for Governance-Embedded, Value-Oriented Digital Health Innovation**

---

## Abstract

Digital health innovations hold transformative potential for healthcare systems worldwide, yet the majority fail to achieve sustainable implementation and scale. This rationale presents the theoretical foundations underpinning the Value-Based Digital Health Innovation Canvas (VDHIC)—a structured visual tool designed to guide digital health innovators through the complete innovation journey while ensuring governance integration and value-based outcomes. Drawing upon theories from strategic management, design thinking, implementation science, responsible innovation, and healthcare quality improvement, this document articulates why a healthcare-specific innovation canvas is essential. The VDHIC synthesizes these multidisciplinary perspectives into an actionable framework anchored by the Quintuple Aim (population health, patient experience, provider satisfaction, cost reduction, and health equity) and contextualized for Indonesia's digital health ecosystem. This rationale establishes the scholarly foundation for the canvas architecture and its potential contribution to improving digital health innovation success rates.

**Keywords:** digital health innovation, business model canvas, Quintuple Aim, responsible innovation, implementation science, governance-by-design, Indonesia, SATUSEHAT

---

## 1\. Introduction: The Digital Health Innovation Challenge

### 1.1 The Promise and Peril of Digital Health

Digital health technologies—encompassing mobile health applications, telemedicine platforms, wearable devices, clinical decision support systems, and artificial intelligence-driven diagnostics—represent one of the most significant opportunities to transform healthcare delivery in the twenty-first century. The World Health Organization (2021), in its *Global Strategy on Digital Health 2020-2025*, identified digital health as essential for achieving universal health coverage and improving health outcomes globally.

Yet beneath this promise lies a sobering reality: the vast majority of digital health innovations fail to achieve meaningful adoption, sustainable implementation, or scalable impact. Industry analyses consistently report that digital health startups face failure rates exceeding 70%, with many promising initiatives struggling to move beyond pilot phases (Research2Guidance, 2023; Rock Health, 2024). The phenomenon termed "pilotitis"—where innovations demonstrate promising results in controlled settings but fail to translate into routine practice—has become endemic in digital health (Greenhalgh et al., 2017).

### 1.2 The Innovation-Implementation Gap

The persistent gap between digital health innovation and successful implementation reflects multiple interacting factors. Greenhalgh et al. (2017), in their development of the NASSS (Non-adoption, Abandonment, Scale-up, Spread, and Sustainability) framework, identified seven domains that influence technology adoption in healthcare: the condition or illness, the technology itself, the value proposition, the adopter system (comprising professional staff, patients, and lay caregivers), the organization(s), the wider (institutional and societal) context, and the interaction and mutual adaptation between all these domains over time.

What emerges from implementation science research is that digital health innovation is fundamentally different from innovation in other sectors. Healthcare operates within a complex adaptive system characterized by multiple interdependent stakeholders, stringent regulatory requirements, asymmetric information between providers and patients, third-party payment structures, professional autonomy, and life-or-death stakes (Plsek & Greenhalgh, 2001). Generic innovation frameworks developed for consumer technology or general business contexts often fail to account for these unique characteristics.

### 1.3 Purpose of This Rationale

This document provides the academic rationale for the Value-Based Digital Health Innovation Canvas (VDHIC)—a structured tool designed to address the shortcomings of existing innovation frameworks when applied to digital health. The VDHIC integrates insights from multiple theoretical traditions to create a healthcare-specific innovation canvas that embeds governance considerations from inception and anchors all innovation efforts to comprehensive value creation through the Quintuple Aim framework.

The following sections establish the theoretical foundations underlying the canvas architecture, articulate the case for a healthcare-specific approach, justify each component of the VDHIC design, and contextualize the framework for Indonesia's digital health ecosystem.

## 2\. Theoretical Foundations

The VDHIC draws upon six major theoretical streams that collectively inform its architecture and application.

### 2.1 Visual Strategy Tools and the Business Model Canvas Legacy

The VDHIC builds upon the visual strategy tool tradition exemplified by the Business Model Canvas (BMC) developed by Osterwalder and Pigneur (2010). The BMC, co-created with contributions from 470 practitioners across 45 countries, revolutionized strategic planning by translating complex business model concepts into a single-page visual representation comprising nine building blocks: customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partnerships, and cost structure.

The BMC's power lies not merely in its content but in its format. As a boundary object (Star & Griesemer, 1989), the canvas creates shared understanding across diverse stakeholders who may otherwise struggle to communicate across disciplinary and organizational boundaries. Star and Griesemer (1989), in their seminal work published in *Social Studies of Science*, defined boundary objects as artifacts that are "both adaptable to different viewpoints and robust enough to maintain identity across them" (p. 393). The visual, workshop-friendly format of canvas tools facilitates collaborative strategy development and enables rapid iteration—qualities essential for innovation processes.

However, the BMC was designed as an industry-agnostic tool, and its application to healthcare contexts reveals significant limitations. The canvas assumes market dynamics—direct customer relationships, price-based competition, and relatively unregulated environments—that do not reflect healthcare realities. Healthcare innovations must navigate third-party payers, regulatory approval processes, clinical evidence requirements, and professional adoption dynamics that the original BMC does not address.

The VDHIC retains the visual canvas format and collaborative workshop orientation that made the BMC successful while redesigning the building blocks to reflect healthcare-specific requirements.

### 2.2 Design Thinking and Human-Centered Innovation

Design thinking, as articulated by Brown (2008) in the *Harvard Business Review*, provides a problem-solving methodology that emphasizes deep empathy with users, iterative prototyping, and tolerance for ambiguity. Brown described design thinking as "a discipline that uses the designer's sensibility and methods to match people's needs with what is technologically feasible and what a viable business strategy can convert into customer value and market opportunity" (p. 86). The design thinking process typically progresses through phases of empathizing, defining, ideating, prototyping, and testing—a non-linear journey that prioritizes understanding human needs before developing solutions.

The VDHIC's three-phase structure (Discover → Design → Deliver) reflects design thinking principles. The Discover phase emphasizes understanding the health challenge, mapping user profiles, and identifying stakeholders before any solution development occurs. This sequencing operationalizes the design thinking insight that premature solution focus is a primary cause of innovation failure.

Human-centered design, as codified in ISO 9241-210, further informs the VDHIC's explicit attention to user experience and adoption. The technology acceptance literature, originating with Davis's (1989) Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), demonstrates that perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use are critical determinants of technology adoption. Davis (1989) found that these two factors explained a significant portion of variance in user acceptance intentions. The VDHIC's User Experience block ensures these factors receive systematic attention during the design phase.

### 2.3 From Triple Aim to Quintuple Aim: Evolving Healthcare Value

The Institute for Healthcare Improvement's (IHI) Triple Aim framework, introduced by Berwick et al. (2008), established three interdependent goals for healthcare system optimization: improving the patient experience of care, improving the health of populations, and reducing the per capita cost of healthcare. The authors argued that "improving the U.S. health care system requires simultaneous pursuit of three aims" (p. 759), and that no single aim could be achieved sustainably without attention to the others. The Triple Aim provided a unifying framework that transcended traditional quality improvement silos and oriented healthcare organizations toward comprehensive value creation.

Bodenheimer and Sinsky (2014) proposed expanding the Triple Aim to a Quadruple Aim by adding a fourth dimension: improving the work life of healthcare providers. Their argument recognized that provider burnout, dissatisfaction, and turnover undermine the achievement of the other three aims. The authors noted that "burnout is associated with lower patient satisfaction, reduced health outcomes, and it may increase costs. Burnout thus imperils the Triple Aim" (p. 573). Healthcare workers experiencing burnout provide lower-quality care, have higher error rates, and leave the profession at elevated rates—creating a vicious cycle that degrades system performance.

Most recently, Nundy et al. (2022) advanced the Quintuple Aim by adding health equity as an explicit fifth dimension. Published in *JAMA*, their argument contended that healthcare systems pursuing the original aims without explicit attention to equity could inadvertently widen disparities. Digital health innovations, in particular, risk exacerbating the "digital divide" if they are designed primarily for affluent, digitally literate populations while leaving underserved communities behind.

The VDHIC adopts the Quintuple Aim as its value anchor, requiring innovators to articulate how their solutions contribute to all five dimensions: population health, patient experience, provider satisfaction, cost reduction, and health equity. This orientation ensures that digital health innovations are designed with comprehensive value creation in mind from inception.

### 2.4 Responsible Innovation and Governance-by-Design

Responsible innovation, as conceptualized by Stilgoe et al. (2013) in *Research Policy*, provides a framework for anticipating and addressing the societal implications of innovation proactively rather than reactively. The framework identifies four dimensions of responsible innovation practice: anticipation (systematic thinking about potential impacts), reflexivity (critical examination of underlying assumptions and values), inclusion (engagement of diverse stakeholders), and responsiveness (capacity to adapt based on emerging knowledge). Stilgoe et al. (2013) argued that "responsible innovation means taking care of the future through collective stewardship of science and innovation in the present" (p. 1570).

The VDHIC operationalizes responsible innovation through its Governance Spine—a structural element that positions regulatory, clinical, data, and technology governance considerations as foundational rather than peripheral concerns. This "governance-by-design" approach contrasts with traditional innovation processes where compliance is addressed only after product development, often resulting in costly redesigns or outright failures when regulatory requirements cannot be met.

The governance-by-design philosophy aligns with Cavoukian's (2011) Privacy by Design framework, which articulates seven foundational principles including "proactive not reactive" and "privacy as the default setting." Cavoukian (2011) emphasized that privacy protection "must be embedded into the design and architecture of IT systems and business practices" (p. 2\) rather than added as an afterthought. Applied to digital health, these principles suggest that data protection, clinical safety, and regulatory compliance should be embedded into innovation processes from the earliest stages.

### 2.5 Implementation Science and Contextual Adaptation

Implementation science—the study of methods to promote the systematic uptake of research findings and evidence-based practices into routine practice—provides crucial insights for digital health innovation. The Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR), developed by Damschroder et al. (2009), identifies five domains influencing implementation success: intervention characteristics, outer setting, inner setting, characteristics of individuals, and the implementation process itself.

The CFIR framework emphasizes that implementation success depends not only on intervention quality but on the fit between the intervention and its implementation context. Damschroder et al. (2009) noted that "the CFIR provides a pragmatic structure for approaching complex, interacting, multi-level, and transient states of constructs in the real world" (p. 2). This insight is particularly relevant for digital health innovations developed in one context (often high-income countries) and deployed in another (often low- and middle-income countries with different infrastructure, workforce, and regulatory environments).

The VDHIC incorporates implementation science insights through its explicit attention to stakeholder mapping, scale strategy, and contextual factors such as SATUSEHAT integration requirements specific to Indonesia. The canvas prompts innovators to consider implementation challenges early in the design process rather than treating implementation as an afterthought.

The RE-AIM framework (Glasgow et al., 1999), which evaluates interventions across five dimensions (Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, and Maintenance), further informs the VDHIC's Deliver phase. Glasgow et al. (1999) argued that "failure to adequately evaluate programs on all 5 dimensions can lead to a waste of resources, discontinuities between stages of research, and failure to improve public health" (p. 1322). This comprehensive evaluation approach ensures that innovators plan for sustainable implementation rather than merely initial deployment.

### 2.6 Sociotechnical Systems Theory

Sociotechnical systems theory, originating in the work of researchers at the Tavistock Institute (Trist, 1981), recognizes that organizations comprise interdependent social and technical subsystems that must be jointly optimized. Trist (1981) articulated that "the technological component of an organization affects the social system and that both must be considered together in any process of organizational design" (p. 24). Applying sociotechnical principles to healthcare, scholars have demonstrated that health information technology implementations frequently fail when they address technical requirements without adequate attention to the social systems—workflows, professional relationships, organizational cultures—into which technologies are introduced.

Plsek and Greenhalgh (2001), in their influential *BMJ* series on complexity in healthcare, argued that healthcare systems exhibit characteristics of complex adaptive systems: "agents within the system... are free to act in ways that are not always totally predictable, and... the actions of one agent changes the context for other agents" (p. 625). This perspective has profound implications for digital health innovation, suggesting that linear, predictable implementation pathways are the exception rather than the rule.

The VDHIC reflects sociotechnical systems thinking through its integration of technical considerations (solution architecture, system integration, technology governance) with social considerations (user profiles, stakeholder mapping, clinical governance, change management). The canvas does not treat technology as an independent variable to be optimized in isolation but as an element of a complex sociotechnical system that must be designed holistically.

## 3\. The Case for a Healthcare-Specific Innovation Canvas

### 3.1 Limitations of Generic Innovation Frameworks

Generic innovation frameworks, despite their widespread adoption, exhibit systematic blind spots when applied to healthcare contexts. The Business Model Canvas assumes direct customer relationships where value is exchanged for payment—a dynamic that rarely applies in healthcare, where patients (the recipients of care), providers (the deliverers of care), and payers (insurance systems or governments) occupy distinct roles with potentially divergent interests (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010).

Lean Startup methodology, with its emphasis on rapid iteration and minimum viable products, can conflict with the patient safety requirements and regulatory approval processes that govern healthcare innovation. The "move fast and break things" ethos celebrated in consumer technology is inappropriate—and potentially dangerous—when applied to medical products and services.

Design thinking frameworks, while valuable for their human-centered orientation, often underemphasize the regulatory, evidentiary, and governance dimensions that are critical in healthcare. A beautifully designed digital health application that fails to meet regulatory requirements or lacks clinical evidence will not achieve adoption regardless of its user experience quality.

### 3.2 Unique Characteristics of Healthcare Innovation

Healthcare innovation operates within a distinctive set of constraints and requirements that necessitate a sector-specific approach:

**Regulatory Complexity:** Medical devices, including software as a medical device (SaMD), must navigate complex regulatory pathways that vary by jurisdiction and risk classification. In Indonesia, this includes BPOM registration requirements and the emerging Regulatory Sandbox for Digital Health Innovation established by the Ministry of Health.

**Evidentiary Requirements:** Unlike consumer products, healthcare innovations often require clinical evidence demonstrating safety, efficacy, and cost-effectiveness before achieving widespread adoption and reimbursement. The hierarchy of evidence in medicine places different weights on different study designs, with randomized controlled trials traditionally considered the gold standard.

**Multi-Stakeholder Dynamics:** Healthcare decisions involve patients, family caregivers, clinicians, administrators, payers, regulators, and policymakers—each with distinct interests, incentive structures, and decision-making authority. Greenhalgh et al. (2017) noted that the "adopter system" in healthcare is inherently complex, comprising multiple interconnected actors whose decisions influence one another.

**Professional Autonomy:** Clinicians exercise significant autonomy in care decisions, meaning that technology adoption depends heavily on professional acceptance and integration into clinical workflows. Bodenheimer and Sinsky (2014) emphasized that digital tools adding to clinician burden ultimately fail, regardless of their technical sophistication.

**Life-and-Death Stakes:** Healthcare innovations can directly affect patient safety, creating ethical and legal requirements for rigorous quality assurance that exceed those in most other sectors. Scally and Donaldson (1998), in their foundational work on clinical governance, argued that healthcare organizations must create "an environment in which excellence in clinical care will flourish" (p. 61\) through systematic attention to quality and safety.

**Payment Complexity:** Healthcare payment systems—including Indonesia's BPJS Kesehatan national health insurance—operate through mechanisms (diagnosis-related groups, capitation, fee-for-service) that shape innovation viability in ways that generic business models do not address. Porter (2010) argued that healthcare value must be defined as "health outcomes achieved per dollar spent" (p. 2477), a framing that requires understanding both the numerator (outcomes) and denominator (costs) within specific payment contexts.

### 3.3 The Need for Integrated Governance

A defining characteristic of the VDHIC is its treatment of governance as integral to innovation rather than external to it. Traditional innovation processes often treat regulatory compliance, clinical governance, data protection, and technology management as separate workstreams addressed by specialized functions after core product development is complete.

This sequential approach creates several problems. First, it risks fundamental design decisions that prove incompatible with governance requirements, necessitating costly late-stage redesigns. Second, it fails to leverage governance considerations as design inputs that can strengthen innovation quality. Third, it perpetuates organizational silos that impede the cross-functional collaboration necessary for successful digital health innovation.

The VDHIC's Governance Spine—comprising Regulatory Pathway, Clinical Governance, Data Governance, and Technology Governance—positions these considerations as foundational elements that inform all other canvas components. This architecture reflects the responsible innovation principle of anticipation (Stilgoe et al., 2013\) while ensuring that governance expertise is engaged throughout the innovation process rather than consulted only at approval gates.

## 4\. VDHIC Architecture: Theoretical Justification

The VDHIC comprises thirteen blocks organized across four structural layers: the Governance Spine (four blocks), the Discovery Phase (three blocks), the Design Phase (three blocks), the Delivery Phase (three blocks), and the Value Anchor (five Quintuple Aim outcomes). This section provides theoretical justification for each component.

### 4.1 The Governance Spine: Embedding Compliance from Inception

The Governance Spine operationalizes responsible innovation and governance-by-design principles through four integrated domains:

**Regulatory Pathway** addresses the external regulatory environment, including medical device classification, regulatory sandbox participation, local content requirements, and certification standards. This block draws on regulatory science frameworks that emphasize early engagement with regulatory requirements as a strategy for reducing approval risk and time-to-market.

**Clinical Governance** addresses patient safety and clinical quality assurance, including clinical protocols, professional oversight, adverse event reporting, and alignment with clinical guidelines. This block draws on the clinical governance framework articulated by Scally and Donaldson (1998), who defined clinical governance as "a system through which NHS organisations are accountable for continuously improving the quality of their services" (p. 61).

**Data Governance** addresses information management, privacy protection, and data quality, including compliance with data protection regulations (Indonesia's UU PDP and PP 71/2019), consent management, data minimization, and security standards. This block draws on information governance frameworks and Cavoukian's (2011) Privacy by Design principles, which emphasize embedding privacy into systems architecture from inception.

**Technology Governance** addresses system architecture, cybersecurity, interoperability, and operational reliability, including disaster recovery, change management, and technical debt management. This block draws on IT governance frameworks and enterprise architecture methodologies that emphasize proactive management of technology assets and risks.

### 4.2 The Discovery Phase: Problem-Driven Innovation

The Discovery Phase comprises three blocks that ensure innovation is grounded in genuine health needs and contextual understanding:

**Health Challenge** requires articulation of the specific health problem being addressed, the current patient journey and pain points, epidemiological data supporting the need, barriers that have prevented previous solutions, and alignment with national health priorities. This block operationalizes design thinking's empathy phase as described by Brown (2008).

**User Profiles** requires identification and characterization of primary and secondary users, including demographics, digital literacy levels, behavioral patterns, motivations, and constraints. This block draws on persona methodology and technology acceptance research, particularly Davis's (1989) emphasis on perceived usefulness and ease of use as determinants of adoption.

**Stakeholder Map** requires systematic identification of all stakeholders who influence or are affected by the innovation, including government agencies, clinical organizations, payers, industry partners, and academic institutions. This block operationalizes implementation science's attention to the outer and inner settings that shape implementation success (Damschroder et al., 2009).

### 4.3 The Design Phase: Solution Architecture and Integration

The Design Phase comprises three blocks that translate discovery insights into concrete solution specifications:

**Solution Architecture** requires specification of the digital health pillar (medicine, diagnostics, therapeutics, or wellness), mechanism of action, device classification, risk level, and digital biomarkers or endpoints. This block draws on digital health taxonomy frameworks and risk-based approaches to medical device regulation.

**User Experience** requires attention to core functionality, usability standards, accessibility requirements, engagement strategies, and technical platform selection. This block draws on usability engineering principles and the technology acceptance model's emphasis on perceived ease of use (Davis, 1989).

**System Integration** (SATUSEHAT Integration in the Indonesian context) requires planning for interoperability with national health information infrastructure, including FHIR resource implementation, HL7 standards compliance, and integration with existing systems. This block draws on health information exchange frameworks emphasizing that digital health value depends on connectivity within the broader health system ecosystem.

### 4.4 The Delivery Phase: Evidence, Scale, and Sustainability

The Delivery Phase comprises three blocks that address the transition from design to sustainable implementation:

**Evidence Plan** requires specification of the evidence generation pathway (pilot studies, randomized controlled trials, real-world evidence), clinical endpoints, study designs, cost-effectiveness analysis approaches, and evidence timelines. This block draws on evidence-based medicine hierarchies and the growing recognition of real-world evidence as essential for digital health evaluation.

**Scale Strategy** requires planning for geographic expansion, change management, training and support models, and the pathway from pilot to national scale. This block draws on diffusion of innovation theory, which identifies attributes affecting adoption rates (relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, observability), and the RE-AIM framework's emphasis on reach, adoption, implementation, and maintenance (Glasgow et al., 1999).

**Business Model** requires specification of revenue models, reimbursement pathways, unit economics, funding strategies, and financial sustainability plans. This block draws on business model theory (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010\) while adapting it for healthcare payment structures, including national health insurance reimbursement mechanisms.

### 4.5 The Value Anchor: Quintuple Aim Orientation

The Value Anchor requires innovators to articulate how their solution contributes to each dimension of the Quintuple Aim:

**Population Health:** What measurable health outcomes will improve at the population level? This dimension draws on public health theory and population health management frameworks, recognizing that individual clinical outcomes must aggregate to population-level improvements.

**Patient Experience:** How will patient satisfaction, engagement, and care experience improve? This dimension draws on patient-centered care frameworks and experience measurement methodologies, acknowledging that patient perceptions of care quality matter alongside clinical outcomes.

**Provider Satisfaction:** How will the innovation improve clinician workflows and reduce burden rather than adding to it? This dimension draws on the burnout literature and Bodenheimer and Sinsky's (2014) argument that care of the patient requires care of the provider.

**Cost Reduction:** What efficiency gains, cost avoidances, or resource optimizations will the innovation generate? This dimension draws on health economics and Porter's (2010) value-based healthcare theory, which defines value as outcomes relative to costs.

**Health Equity:** How will the innovation reduce rather than exacerbate healthcare disparities? This dimension draws on health equity frameworks as articulated by Braveman (2006), who defined health equity as "the absence of systematic disparities in health... between social groups who have different levels of underlying social advantage/disadvantage" (p. 167), and Nundy et al.'s (2022) argument for explicit equity attention in healthcare improvement.

## 5\. Contextual Adaptation: The Indonesian Digital Health Ecosystem

### 5.1 LMIC Considerations in Digital Health

Low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) face distinctive challenges and opportunities in digital health innovation. Infrastructure limitations (connectivity, electricity, device availability), workforce constraints (limited health informatics expertise), and resource scarcity create contexts that differ substantially from the high-income settings where many digital health frameworks originate.

At the same time, LMICs may enjoy advantages including less entrenched legacy systems, younger and more digitally native populations, and political will to leverage technology for health system strengthening. The phenomenon of "leapfrogging"—adopting advanced technologies without passing through intermediate stages—has been observed in LMIC digital health contexts.

The VDHIC acknowledges these dynamics by incorporating considerations such as offline functionality, low-bandwidth optimization, and accessibility for populations with limited digital literacy—factors that global frameworks often overlook.

### 5.2 Indonesia's Regulatory and Policy Landscape

Indonesia's digital health ecosystem operates within a specific regulatory and policy context that the VDHIC addresses explicitly:

**BPOM (Badan Pengawas Obat dan Makanan)** serves as the national regulatory authority for medical devices, including software as a medical device. Understanding BPOM classification pathways and registration requirements is essential for any digital health innovation targeting the Indonesian market.

**The Regulatory Sandbox for Digital Health Innovation**, established by the Ministry of Health, provides a controlled environment for testing innovative digital health solutions before full regulatory approval. The VDHIC is designed to support sandbox application processes by ensuring comprehensive documentation of innovation characteristics, governance arrangements, and value propositions.

**UU PDP (Undang-Undang Pelindungan Data Pribadi)** establishes Indonesia's data protection framework, with specific implications for health data handling, consent requirements, and cross-border data transfers. The VDHIC's Data Governance block ensures compliance with these requirements.

**BPJS Kesehatan**, Indonesia's national health insurance program covering over 200 million beneficiaries, shapes the financial viability of digital health innovations through its reimbursement mechanisms (INACBGs for hospitals, capitation for primary care). The VDHIC's Business Model block addresses these payment pathway considerations.

### 5.3 SATUSEHAT and National Health Information Architecture

SATUSEHAT represents Indonesia's national health information exchange platform, mandating interoperability among healthcare providers and digital health applications. Compliance with SATUSEHAT requirements—including FHIR resource implementation and standardized data exchange—is increasingly essential for digital health innovations operating in Indonesia.

The VDHIC treats SATUSEHAT integration not as an optional consideration but as a core design requirement reflected in a dedicated canvas block. This emphasis recognizes that innovations failing to integrate with national health information infrastructure will face significant barriers to adoption and scale regardless of their standalone quality.

## 6\. Synthesis: Why VDHIC is Essential

### 6.1 Addressing the Innovation-Governance Paradox

Digital health innovation faces a fundamental paradox: the agility and iteration that characterize successful innovation processes can conflict with the stability and assurance that governance frameworks require. Move too fast, and regulatory or safety failures derail innovation. Move too slowly, and innovation loses relevance or momentum.

The VDHIC addresses this paradox by integrating governance into the innovation process itself rather than positioning governance as an external constraint. The Governance Spine ensures that regulatory, clinical, data, and technology governance considerations inform design decisions from inception, reducing the likelihood of late-stage conflicts between innovation ambitions and governance requirements.

This integration reflects the responsible innovation principle of anticipation—systematic thinking about potential implications before they materialize (Stilgoe et al., 2013)—while maintaining the iterative, human-centered orientation that design thinking provides (Brown, 2008).

### 6.2 Enabling Multi-Stakeholder Alignment

Digital health innovation requires alignment across diverse stakeholders: clinical champions who will adopt the technology, administrators who will fund implementation, regulators who will approve market entry, payers who will reimburse services, and patients who will ultimately benefit. These stakeholders often operate with different vocabularies, priorities, and decision-making frameworks.

The VDHIC functions as a boundary object (Star & Griesemer, 1989)—an artifact that facilitates communication and collaboration across communities of practice. The visual canvas format creates shared understanding that enables productive dialogue among stakeholders who might otherwise struggle to communicate effectively.

Workshop-based facilitation using the VDHIC can surface assumptions, identify conflicts, and build consensus in ways that traditional document-based planning processes cannot achieve. This collaborative orientation reflects the responsible innovation principle of inclusion—meaningful engagement of diverse stakeholders in innovation processes (Stilgoe et al., 2013).

### 6.3 Operationalizing Value-Based Healthcare

The value-based healthcare movement, advanced by Porter (2010), calls for orienting healthcare systems around patient outcomes relative to the costs of achieving those outcomes. Yet operationalizing value-based healthcare in innovation processes remains challenging. Innovators may default to measuring what is easily measurable (downloads, engagement metrics) rather than what matters (health outcomes, equity impacts).

The VDHIC operationalizes value-based healthcare by anchoring all innovation efforts to the Quintuple Aim. The Value Anchor requires innovators to articulate specific contributions to population health, patient experience, provider satisfaction, cost reduction, and health equity—ensuring that value creation is designed in rather than assessed after the fact.

This orientation also supports the growing emphasis on outcomes-based reimbursement and value-based contracting in healthcare payment reform. Innovations designed with explicit outcome commitments are better positioned to participate in emerging payment models that reward demonstrated value.

---

## 7\. Conclusion

The Value-Based Digital Health Innovation Canvas represents a synthesis of multiple theoretical traditions—visual strategy tools (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010), design thinking (Brown, 2008), the Quintuple Aim (Nundy et al., 2022), responsible innovation (Stilgoe et al., 2013), implementation science (Damschroder et al., 2009; Glasgow et al., 1999), and sociotechnical systems theory (Trist, 1981; Plsek & Greenhalgh, 2001)—into an integrated framework purpose-built for digital health innovation in complex healthcare environments.

The canvas addresses critical gaps in existing innovation frameworks by embedding governance from inception, anchoring innovation to comprehensive value creation, and contextualizing for specific regulatory and health system environments. Its visual, workshop-friendly format enables the multi-stakeholder collaboration essential for navigating healthcare's complexity.

For Indonesia's digital health ecosystem, the VDHIC provides a structured pathway for innovation that aligns with national priorities including SATUSEHAT implementation, regulatory sandbox participation, and universal health coverage through BPJS Kesehatan. By ensuring that innovations address the Quintuple Aim—including explicit attention to provider satisfaction and health equity—the canvas supports the development of digital health solutions that create sustainable value for all stakeholders.

The theoretical foundations articulated in this rationale establish the scholarly basis for the VDHIC architecture. Empirical validation through application in diverse innovation contexts will further refine the framework and generate evidence for its effectiveness in improving digital health innovation success rates.
