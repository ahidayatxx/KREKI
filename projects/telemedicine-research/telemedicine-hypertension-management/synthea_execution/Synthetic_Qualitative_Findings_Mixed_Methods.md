## 2. Mock Interview Transcripts

### 2.1 Semi-Structured Patient Interview

**Interviewer**: Dr. Sarah Chen, Research Clinician
**Patient**: "Linda W.", 61 years old, high engagement archetype
**Date**: October 25, 2025
**Method**: Video teleconference

**Interview Transcript**:

**Q: Can you tell me about your experience with the telemedicine program for your hypertension?**

**A**: "Overall, it's been really good, much better than having to go to the doctor all the time. I like that I can check my blood pressure at home and the doctor can see it right away. The app is pretty easy to use, though sometimes I forget how to sync the readings."

**Q: What do you find most challenging about managing your blood pressure remotely?**

**A**: "Honestly, it's the not knowing. When my pressure is high, I worry, but I have to wait for someone to call me back. Sometimes that takes a day or two. And I still don't understand why it's high sometimes even when I'm doing everything right."

**Q: How has this affected your daily life and relationships?**

**A**: "My husband is very supportive, he helps me remember to take my medicines. But I think I worry more now because I'm monitoring it so closely. Before, I only knew my pressure when I went to the doctor every few months. Now I see it every day, and the ups and downs are stressful."

**Q: What suggestions do you have for improving the program?**

**A**: "I'd like more education about what causes the fluctuations. Like, is it stress? Diet? The weather? And maybe quicker responses when my readings are high. And support groups - I think it would help to talk to other people going through the same thing."

### 2.2 Healthcare Provider Interview

**Interviewer**: Dr. Michael Torres, Qualitative Researcher
**Provider**: Dr. Jennifer Park, Primary Care Physician
**Date**: October 26, 2025
**Method**: Video teleconference

**Interview Transcript**:

**Q: What are your observations about patient engagement with the telemedicine hypertension program?**

**A**: "It's really mixed. About half of my patients love it - they're engaged, they track everything, they're proactive. The other half struggle, either with technology or with the self-management aspect. The interesting thing is that engagement doesn't always correlate with blood pressure control. Some of my most engaged patients still have uncontrolled hypertension."

**Q: What challenges do you face in providing care through telemedicine?**

**A**: "The biggest challenge is the lack of physical cues and the inability to do immediate interventions. When a patient comes in with high blood pressure in person, I can talk to them, adjust medications, maybe even admit them if needed. With telemedicine, there's a delay. Also, I can't assess things like edema or listen to their heart sounds remotely."

**Q: How has this changed your practice?**

**A**: "I've become more dependent on patient-reported data and less on physical examination. I've also had to develop new skills in remote patient education and motivation. And I spend more time coordinating with the telemedicine team - it's really a team-based approach now."

**Q: What do you think would improve the program?**

**A**: "Better integration with the electronic health record would help. Also, more decision support tools for the telemedicine team. And we need better protocols for when to escalate care versus when to manage remotely. Sometimes it's hard to know when a patient needs to come in person."

### 3.2 Provider Focus Group: Telemedicine Implementation Challenges

**Date**: October 29, 2025
**Participants**: 4 healthcare providers (2 physicians, 1 nurse practitioner, 1 pharmacist)
**Moderator**: Dr. Michael Torres
**Setting**: Virtual focus group via video conference

**Discussion Highlights**:

**Dr. Park (Physician)**: "The biggest challenge I've encountered is identifying which patients are suitable for telemedicine management versus those who need in-person care."

**Nurse Practitioner Garcia**: "And workload management. We're seeing more patients overall because telemedicine visits are shorter, but the follow-up and monitoring between visits takes a lot of time."

**Dr. Chen (Physician)**: "I'm concerned about clinical decision-making without physical examination. Yesterday I had a patient with elevated readings and headache symptoms. I couldn't be sure if it was just uncontrolled hypertension or something more serious."

**Pharmacist Lee**: "From a medication perspective, it's been interesting. We can monitor adherence better through the pharmacy refill data linked to the telemedicine system, but we're seeing more side effect reports because patients are more aware of their blood pressure fluctuations."

**Dr. Park**: "I think we need better protocols. Sometimes I'm not sure when to have a patient come in versus when to manage remotely. There's a gray area that's challenging."

**Nurse Practitioner Garcia**: "And we need better training for patients. Some patients don't understand how to properly measure blood pressure at home, leading to inaccurate readings."

**Dr. Chen**: "The technology itself is also a barrier for some patients. We've had to create different levels of support - basic phone calls, video visits, full remote monitoring. It's like we're running multiple programs simultaneously."

**Pharmacist Lee**: "But despite the challenges, I think we're reaching patients who might not otherwise engage with care. Some of my patients who never came to in-person visits are now regularly checking in via telemedicine."

## 6. Coding Scheme Test Results

### 6.1 Initial Coding Application

**Coder**: Research Team
**Date**: November 2, 2025
**Material**: Synthetic qualitative data provided above

**Applied Codes**:

#### **Technology-Related Codes**
- **TECH_ANXIETY**: Patient anxiety about using technology
- **TECH_BARRIER**: Technical difficulties preventing engagement
- **TECH_FACILITATOR**: Technology enabling care access
- **TECH_SUPPORT**: Need for technical assistance

#### **Clinical Care Codes**
- **BP_CONTROL_FRUSTRATION**: Emotional distress from uncontrolled BP
- **MED_ADHERENCE_BARRIER**: Factors preventing medication adherence
- **CLINICAL_DECISION_MAKING**: Uncertainty about treatment approaches
- **PHYSICAL_EXAM_LIMITATION**: Limitations of remote care

#### **Psychosocial Codes**
- **FAMILY_SUPPORT**: Family involvement in care
- **SOCIAL_ISOLATION**: Lack of support system
- **STRESS_IMPACT**: Stress affecting health outcomes
- **HEALTH_LITERACY**: Patient understanding of health information

#### **System-Level Codes**
- **WORKFLOW_INTEGRATION**: Integration challenges in clinical practice
- **RESOURCE_ALLOCATION**: Distribution of healthcare resources
- **PROTOCOL_DEVELOPMENT**: Need for standardized approaches
- **TEAM_COMMUNICATION**: Interprofessional collaboration

#### **Patient Experience Codes**
- **CONVENIENCE**: Benefits of telemedicine accessibility
- **PERSONALIZED_CARE**: Desire for individualized approaches
- **IMMEDIATE_FEEDBACK**: Need for rapid response to concerns
- **EDUCATIONAL_NEEDS**: Gaps in patient understanding

### 6.2 Coding Refinement Insights

**Challenges Identified**:
1. **Code Overlap**: Some quotes fit multiple categories (e.g., technology barriers and health literacy)
2. **Context Dependence**: Meaning varies by patient archetype
3. **Temporal Elements**: Codes need to capture changes over time
4. **Emotional Content**: Need better codes for emotional responses

**Recommended Code Additions**:
- **EMOTIONAL_BURDEN**: Psychological impact of chronic disease management
- **TEMPORAL_FRUSTRATION**: Frustration with slow progress over time
- **CARE_TRANSITION**: Movement between telemedicine and in-person care
- **DIGITAL_DIVIDE**: Socioeconomic factors affecting technology access

## 7. Triangulation Framework Practice

### 7.1 Quantitative-Qualitative Integration Examples

#### **Example 1: High Engagement, Uncontrolled BP**

**Quantitative Data**: 22 patients with frequent readings (avg 15+ readings/month), multiple medication changes, persistent elevated BP (avg 155/95)

**Qualitative Insights**:
- High motivation and adherence
- Frustration with lack of progress
- Technology comfort but emotional burden
- Family support present

**Integrated Understanding**: These patients are doing everything "right" within the current system but may need additional therapeutic approaches beyond medication optimization (e.g., stress management, specialized testing).

#### **Example 2: Low Engagement Patients**

**Quantitative Data**: 4 patients with infrequent readings (<5 readings/month), missed appointments, minimal medication use

**Qualitative Insights**:
- Technology resistance and anxiety
- Limited support systems
- Forgetfulness and routine disruption
- Preference for traditional care

**Integrated Understanding**: These patients need alternative engagement strategies, possibly including phone-based monitoring, in-person support, or simplified technology solutions.

### 7.2 Mixed-Methods Insights

1. **Engagement ≠ Control**: High engagement doesn't guarantee BP control
2. **Technology Access Varies**: Digital divide affects program reach
3. **Emotional Factors Matter**: Stress and anxiety impact clinical outcomes
4. **Family Support Critical**: Social support influences both engagement and outcomes
5. **Individual Needs Vary**: One-size-fits-all approach insufficient

## 8. Protocol Refinement Recommendations

### 8.1 Data Collection Enhancements

1. **Expanded Interview Guides**: Include questions about emotional burden, family dynamics, technology preferences
2. **Longitudinal Component**: Track changes in patient experiences over time
3. **Family/Caregiver Perspectives**: Include support system members in data collection
4. **Technology Assessment**: Systematic evaluation of technology barriers and facilitators

### 8.2 Integration Framework Improvements

1. **Temporal Analysis**: Map qualitative themes to quantitative trends over time
2. **Subgroup Analysis**: Differentiate integration approaches by patient archetype
3. **Contextual Factors**: Include socioeconomic, cultural, and system-level influences
4. **Outcome Linking**: Directly connect qualitative insights to clinical outcomes

### 8.3 Analytical Enhancements

1. **Joint Displays**: Create visual representations of quantitative-qualitative integration
2. **Case Studies**: Develop detailed patient cases illustrating mixed-methods findings
3. **Process Evaluation**: Assess how telemedicine implementation affects care processes
4. **Stakeholder Triangulation**: Compare perspectives across patient, provider, and system levels

## 9. Conclusions for Protocol Testing

The synthetic qualitative data provided above serves as a realistic testing ground for the mixed-methods integration protocol. Key lessons learned:

1. **Archetype Approach Works**: Patient categorization helps organize and interpret findings
2. **Code Development Needed**: Initial coding scheme requires refinement for emotional and temporal elements
3. **Integration Complexity**: Connecting qualitative insights to quantitative patterns requires systematic approach
4. **Stakeholder Diversity**: Multiple perspectives enrich understanding but complicate analysis
5. **Protocol Flexibility**: Need adaptive approaches for different patient groups and contexts

This synthetic dataset allows researchers to practice and refine their mixed-methods techniques before applying them to real patient data, ensuring a more robust and insightful analysis when the actual study begins.
